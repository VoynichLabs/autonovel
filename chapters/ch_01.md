# Chapter 1

## Raj

Raj woke groggily, staring into three of the six eyes of something that was staring back.

As a long-time addict, he had a depth of experience with waking up in strange places with strange companions. But this was distinctly different. 

The creature raised a tentacle. Trailing from it was a luminous, jellyfish-like thing with long, multicolored tendrils, pulsing softly as if it were breathing light. The tentacle brought it to Raj's face.

The jellyfish passed through some invisible membrane he hadn't noticed before and settled onto him.

Then the whole creature seemed to melt, spreading across his skin like warm, living film. The tendrils passed up through his nose, and he felt them embrace every synapse in his brain. 

His heart rate slowed. His metabolism spiked. Heat surged through his body, sharp and fast, like he'd been injected with panic and calm at the same time.

He felt something growing from his temples.

Two tiny chitinous nubs began to form, pressing outward as if his cerebellum was sprouting horns.

But none of his extremities responded. The primal electrical impulses from his brain hit his body and vanished somewhere along the way, as if the wiring had been cut.

His eyes darted, desperate for context.

The space around him was cavernous and colossal, industrial and alien, lit by a dull glow. And he wasn't alone. Other humans hung suspended in what looked like transparent tubes.

A cloud of translucent, luminescent jellyfish-things swarmed around it, drifting like a living halo. 

 He had seen six so far, but he couldn't shake the feeling there were more.

 

Embracing him.

Claiming him.

Then the room changed.

A massive wall of the chamber—solid one moment—began to liquefy. It didn't open so much as dissolve, the boundary turning into a rippling film.

Something slid through.

Another squid-like creature entered, even more imposing. Its body pulsed with bioluminescent color, hundreds of shades, flashing and flowing across its skin in fractal patterns.

He watched the two squids flash colors at each other—rapid sequences, violent bursts, rippling gradients. 

His mother's voice filled his head, sharp with anger.

The squids were still flashing at each other.

One of them was upset. The larger one—the bluish one—was angry, and Raj could feel the shape of it even without words.

The greenish squid—the one that had placed the jellyfish on Raj—responded in cascading waves of color.

Raj's mind reeled.

*What a strange trip.*

He forced himself to relax. He'd survived worse nights than this. Worse hallucinations.

*Got in trouble at school again?*

*Please don't be the square root of magenta! It's just a little experiment. And besides, if it works, you might finally get answers about those artifacts you're so interested in—*

The other squid responded—

in his ex-girlfriend's voice.

*Fine. Do what you want. But I am not going to lose a dorsal tentacle over this.*

For a moment, both squids shifted to the same off-pink tint, like two moods aligning in reluctant agreement.

Then the bluish squid slid back through the wall-membrane and disappeared.

The green squid turned toward Raj again.

It approached Raj's tube as if it were returning to unfinished work.

Raj felt the tendrils in his brain tighten. Felt the boney electric growths at his temples pulse.

The green squid leaned in, almost close enough to touch, and Raj heard its voice in his head—bright, delighted, predatory. It wasn't the squid, it was his grandmother.

*Oh! Look at you!*

*You're such a smart one, aren't you?*

It flashed softly, a ripple of teal color that felt like affection and possession.

*Let's get you some tentacles.*

*You're going to be my little helper.* 🦑

## Irina

A strange dream of colorful fractals ended the instant Irina's eyes opened.

A giant tentacle hovered inches from her face—luminous, shimmering plasma blobs crawling along it, tendrils branching off in twin spirals like DNA. It drifted closer. Then closer. It didn't push against anything. It simply passed through the enclosure's membrane as if the barrier were fog.

*Сука бляат!*
*Ити нахуй!*
Get the fuck away from me!

She tried to scream, but nothing happened.  Panic didn't need a voice. It detonated through her anyway: fear, rage, disgust, firing down every path her nervous system still remembered having.

  She tried to recoil. There was nowhere to recoil to.

A sudden electrical pop—

Black.

When she surfaced again, vision returned in broken pieces. Color pulsed in the dark: two massive octopus-shapes, impossibly large, flashing bands and ripples like living signals. 

 The cadence was wrong. The words were wrong. The authority was wrong. It sounded like adults speaking over something fragile they didn't think could hear them.

*Hey!! Let me out of here!!!*
*What the hell is going on?*
*What is this?*

The reply came instantly—not through ears, not through air, but placed cleanly inside her mind.

*Shhhh.*

Quick. Distinct.

Her older brother's voice.

*"Тихо!! Mama and papa are fighting. I want to hear what they're saying."*

She tried to locate the source.

It wasn't her brother, but it was something distinctly human.

*"Irishka! You need to be quiet you're going to upset them!"*

## Xi Pei

Xi Pei woke inside something that was not a room.

 

There was a creature outside the tube.

He had worked for enough people who didn't understand what he did to know when he was being observed. The creature was enormous. Eight, maybe nine meters. Its body pulsed color in sequences that had the quality of speech — too patterned to be random, too fast to follow. Four of its eyes were on him. Two tracked elsewhere, monitoring things Xi Pei couldn't yet see.

 He filed that. No immediate escape — he had no data on what was outside — but good to know.

Something drifted past in his peripheral vision.

A phone. An iPhone 13 Pro, matte graphite. It moved without being held, rotating slowly in the ambient current of the space. The edge had a specific micro-scratch pattern he recognized from final QC — not a defect, a tooling artifact from a particular fixture set on Line 7 at the Zhengzhou facility. He had flagged that fixture twice. Maintenance had deprioritized it both times.

He looked away.

The creature outside his tube moved.

A tentacle extended through the membrane — it passed through the wall like the wall was a suggestion — and it was carrying something. Luminous. Fractal. Alive in a way that didn't correspond to any taxonomy he knew. Long tendrils branching and rebranching, pulsing at frequencies that made his eyes feel like they were receiving information they hadn't agreed to process.

Xi Pei watched it approach his face.

He had read somewhere — a management book, years ago, the kind his first factory director had required everyone to read — that panic was information you weren't using. He had considered this largely nonsense at the time. He considered it marginally less nonsense now.

The thing entered him through his nose. There was no other word for what happened. It passed through the cartilage and bone as if these were not obstacles, and then it spread, and then the spreading was everywhere at once, touching every synapse in a sequence that felt less like invasion and more like an audit.

Somewhere at the back of his skull, something popped.

He had expected pain. There was some. Mostly there was the sensation of being read, rapidly, by something that understood the format of the data but not what the data meant. Like being scanned without being comprehended.

Two small growths formed at his temples. Chitinous. He could feel their mass and their shape without touching them. 

The creature withdrew.

Xi Pei observed the space properly now.

Other tubes, other humans, strung across the chamber at irregular intervals. Some were awake. One was making noise he couldn't hear through the membrane but could read in the posture: screaming. Another was limp in a way that might be unconscious or might be something else. A third was — dressed. Outfitted in something that covered the torso and limbs in a material that moved like a second skin, iridescent, segmented along lines that didn't correspond to human anatomy. 

To his left, something happened that he processed in sections.

A small human — six, maybe seven years old, too young to be here, which meant the selection criteria were broader than he'd assumed — was near the outer edge of the chamber's clear wall. Another creature entered the space. Larger than the first. Different color patterning. It moved past the child with the casual efficiency of something that had more important places to be, and then the child was not there anymore.

No dramatics. No pause. The creature continued on its path.

Xi Pei filed it under: ceiling for what they were here.

The growths at his temples pulsed once, sharply, and then something opened — not physically, but in the way a channel opens, the way a frequency finds its receiver — and the room changed quality. Sound without sound. Color that arrived as meaning. Fragments of something structured moving through him the way a logistics update moved through a system: incomplete, contextless, but patterned.

He understood almost none of it.

He noted that he understood almost none of it.

The iPhone drifted past again, closer this time. He tracked it without moving his eyes — a habit from the factory floor, where watching without appearing to watch kept you out of conversations that cost time. Graphite. 13 Pro. Line 7 artifact on the lower right bezel, three millimeters from the power button.
