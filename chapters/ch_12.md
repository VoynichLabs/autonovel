# Chapter 12

The weight of the world shifted.

Cass felt it in his bones first—a change in the pressure of the water around him, a thickening in the medium itself. His father was working the bronze in the eastern kiln, the one built into the quarry face where the stone naturally amplified the heat. Even from inside the house, even with three walls between them and the entrance tunnel, Cass could hear the subsonic hum of the fire singing its way through the metal.

He sat at the kitchen table with a cup of tea going cold. His mother had made it an hour ago, maybe two. Time was slipping sideways again. The needle behind his left eye pulsed in rhythm with the hum—not pain exactly, but something that pain was learning to be. Something that would become pain if he paid attention to it.

He didn't pay attention.

Outside the window, the light was that particular shade of late afternoon that meant the sun was falling toward the water. The world above—the one he remembered in fragments, the one that existed before the ship, before the implant, before the white room where they'd scraped him and filled him—that world had seasons that mattered. Angle of light meant time meant consequence.

Here, the light didn't mean anything. The keel-glow from the ship's interior provided its own illumination, constant and sourceless. The sun was decoration.

His mother came down the stairs. She was wearing the blue dress. The real one. Not the bio-suit layer that had begun to accumulate on her skin like a second membrane—that had been there for weeks, shimmering faintly when she moved, visible if you knew what to look for—but the actual fabric dress, the blue one she'd been wearing when they pulled her out of her kitchen in Oslo. It had been small then. A human thing. Now it hung on her differently.

"Your father wants you down there," she said. Not looking at him. Looking through the window at the light that didn't mean anything.

"I know."

"He's asking specifically. Not just because you're supposed to help. Because he wants—" She stopped. Started again. "There's something he needs to show you about the last piece. About how the sound comes out."

Cass's cup was definitely cold now. The surface had a film on it, the way untouched tea develops its own thin skin. He could see his reflection in it, distorted. Everything looked distorted when you went looking for your own face.

"When?" he said.

"Now."

She stood in the doorway like she was waiting for something. He could see the places where the bio-suit had begun to show through the dress—thin lines of iridescence at the neckline, along the inside of her wrists. They shifted color when the ship's ambient light cycled. Right now they were reading a soft green that made his mother look like she was made of fractured glass.

"I don't want to go down there," Cass said. Not looking at her either. Looking at the cold tea.

His mother didn't respond to that. She turned and walked back to the stairs. He heard her climbing, the rustle of the dress, the sound of her feet on stone, then nothing.

The needle pulsed again. Three beats. Then it settled.

---

The kiln entrance was carved into the stone face of the eastern cliff, which meant it was the part of this place that predated everything else. His father had explained this once, back when explanation was something that happened between them. The cliff was real. The quarry was real. Everything else—the house, the terrariums, the storage vaults where the other specimens floated in their suspension pods—was overlay. Grown over the rock like moss over old wood.

Cass could feel the heat from three hundred feet away. It was in the air, making it thick and harder to breathe. His father's work clothes hung on pegs just inside the entrance—heavy leather things, layered and old, darkened by soot and mineral deposit. Cass had stopped wearing his own work clothes weeks ago. The heat didn't bother him the way it used to. The bio-suit handled temperature regulation now. He could walk into a kiln that would cook a human being from the outside in, and his skin would stay cool.

He wasn't sure when he'd stopped thinking of himself as a human being. Sometime after the implant took. Sometime after he realized that his thoughts weren't quite his thoughts anymore, that there was a layer underneath his thinking that wasn't generated by him. That was the ship. Or the implant. Or the water. Or something else.

His father was at the far end of the kiln chamber, standing in front of the largest piece. Bronze, as tall as Cass himself, shaped like nothing and everything. It had no recognizable form. But it sang. Even standing this far away, even with the kiln fire burning between them and the ambient noise of the ship's systems in the background, Cass could hear what his father had been building toward.

The piece was resonating at a frequency just below the range his ears could quite track. But the implant could track it. The chitinous nubs on either side of his skull were receiving the vibration, translating it into something he could almost understand. Almost.

"Come closer," his father said. He was holding something—a tool, maybe, or a piece of metal that wasn't finished yet. Something was wrong with his arm.

Cass moved through the heat.

As he got closer, he saw that his father's left arm had changed. The skin along the forearm had taken on a translucence that wasn't quite the bio-suit layer. It was something underneath. Something that looked like it was made of the same material as the bronze itself—not metal, but something with the same quality of response to light. When his father moved, the arm caught the fire-light and reflected it in ways that human flesh couldn't.

"Your mother told you what I need," his father said. Not a question.

"About the sound," Cass said. "About how it comes out."

His father nodded. He was looking at the bronze piece. He seemed to be looking at it the way someone looks at their child—recognition, calculation, something like hope.

"The piece is finished," his father said. "Structurally. But I can't complete it alone. I can't hear what I'm hearing. Not precisely. Not in a way I can trust."

He turned to face Cass. That's when Cass saw that his father's eyes were different. The iris was larger. The pupil had that flat quality that meant it had stopped responding to light the way human pupils respond to light. It was doing something else now. Processing something. Seeing something underneath the visible spectrum.

"I need you to listen," his father said. "The way the implant lets you listen. And tell me what the piece wants to sing."

Cass felt the needle behind his eye spike. Not in pain. In recognition.

"I can't," he said.

His father went back to looking at the bronze. He raised the piece he'd been holding—it was shaped like a tuning fork, but with edges that didn't quite align the way human tools aligned—and pressed it against the surface of the larger piece.

The sound that came out wasn't sound. It was a vibration that moved through the air and then through the stone and then through Cass's body like something that had weight. Like he was being physically pushed from the inside.

He fell to one knee.

The implant translated the vibration into something like thought. Something like memory. Something like his father's intention, rendered in pure mathematical space. Cass could feel the shape of it—the piece wasn't finished, but it could be finished, and when it was finished it would sing in a particular key that his father had been chasing for months. Maybe for years. Cass couldn't quite locate where the memory ended and the new information began.

"Do you see it?" his father said.

"Yes," Cass heard himself say. "But it's not what you think it is."

His father's hand, the one attached to the arm that had become something else, paused against the bronze. "Explain," he said.

And Cass did. He explained the harmonic that was one octave below the one his father thought he was pursuing. He explained how the resonance would propagate differently in water than it did in air, how the piece was built for a medium it had never yet been tested in. He explained how when they lowered it—and they would lower it, he knew this now, he knew it the way you know things that are already decided—the sound would change. The bronze itself would respond to the pressure, the temperature, the mineral content of the medium. It would sing at a completely different frequency.

His father listened. He listened the way a man listens when someone is explaining how his life's work needs to be unmade and remade from the inside out.

"Can it still be beautiful?" his father said. "In the water. Can it still be beautiful?"

Cass didn't know. He thought about this. He thought about what beauty meant in a medium where light didn't propagate the way it did in air, where color was only meaningful if you had specialized optical organs, where sound moved at different speeds and distances. Where the entire sensory landscape was alien to anything a human being was built to understand.

"Yes," he said. Because he could feel the piece's potential now. And it was beautiful. But it was a beauty that had nothing to do with what his father had learned to find beautiful. It was a beauty that came from the place where the implant was integrated with his skull, from the part of him that was being remade into something that could live in this water, that could hear in this medium, that could understand what the bronze wanted to be.

His father lowered his hand. He set the tuning fork down on the stone bench beside him.

"Then I need to begin again," his father said.

Cass climbed back to his feet. The needle had settled into a steady pulse now, almost meditative. Almost comfortable. He was developing a tolerance for it the way a body develops a tolerance for anything applied consistently. Soon it would stop registering as pain at all. It would become baseline.

"There's something else," his father said. "Something I need to tell you."

Cass waited. The fire in the kiln was beginning to soften at the edges. The heat was becoming less terrible and more like weather—a thing that happened, that could be survived, that had nothing to do with whether you survived it or not.

"I was contacted," his father said. "By one of the crew. The one that reads in the green frequencies. The one your mother calls Delta. It came to me in the water, in the part of the day when you're all down at the collection apparatus. It told me something."

His father's jaw was working. It was an expression Cass hadn't seen in a long time—the expression of a man trying to say something true when all his usual tools for truth-telling had become inadequate.

"The collection apparatus is ready," his father said. "The nets are prepared. In the next cycle, they're bringing up the final group. Forty-seven, or fifty-two. The crew is conflicted about whether we should be integrated into their operation. We're taking up resources. We're not producing anything measurable. They're asking questions about what our use value might be."

The water around them seemed to thicken. Or maybe that was just how Cass's perception worked now. Fear felt like pressure. Like gravity. Like the weight of something that lived in the darkness below.

"What did it want?" Cass said. "The one in the green frequencies. When it contacted you."

His father looked at the bronze piece. He looked at it the way you look at something you're saying goodbye to.

"It wanted to know if we're worth keeping," his father said.

---

Cass went up the stairs instead of out through the cliff entrance. He didn't want to walk through the heat again. He wanted to move through the house, through the familiar geometry of the place they'd been brought to, the place that was starting to feel less like a prison and more like the only real thing in an increasingly unreal universe.

His mother was in the study. She had the devices spread out on the table—the phones, the things they'd all been carrying when the nets came through the water and pulled them. All six of them had carried these rectangles of glass and metal and silicon. Mirrors. Windows. Doors.

She wasn't touching them. She was sitting at the table looking at them the way his father had been looking at the bronze piece. With recognition and calculation and something like heartbreak.

"He told you," she said.

"Yes."

She reached out and picked up one of the phones. Not her own—Cass could tell because his mother's had been in a turquoise case with a crack in the corner. This one was black. Unmarked. She turned it over in her hands.

"I tried to understand them," she said. "When we first arrived. I thought if I could figure out how they worked, what they were supposed to do, I could bargain with the crew. Tell them something valuable in exchange for... I don't know. Better conditions. A promise. Something."

She put the phone down carefully. It made a small sound when it touched the wood.

"But I don't understand them anymore," she said. "They look like glass and metal, but I can feel that they're made of something else. Something that doesn't respond to the normal laws of how things are built. It's like looking at a tool from a species that thinks about shape completely differently than we do."

Cass understood that. He understood it in a way that had nothing to do with technical knowledge and everything to do with the implant translating what he was seeing into patterns his nervous system could recognize.

"They want to keep us," Cass said. He wasn't sure how he knew this. He just did. The implant was pulling information from somewhere. Not receiving it, not exactly. Inferring it from the background noise of the ship's telepathic infrastructure. Reading the mathematical signatures of crew intent the way his father had taught him to read the sound of bronze responding to heat.

His mother looked at him. She looked at him for a long time. Cass could see the moment something shifted in her expression. The moment she accepted that her son—the boy she'd come from—was becoming something that could understand things she never would. Something that could hear frequencies she'd never have the antennae for.

"Yes," she said. "They want to keep us. But not as pets. Not as specimens. They want to understand what we represent."

She gestured at the phones again. At the impossible artifacts scattered on the table.

"They still don't know how these were built," she said. "How vermin—that's what they call us, you know, when they think we can't understand. Vermin, like rats. How vermin could possibly create something like this. It contradicts every principle of their civilization. It shouldn't be possible. And yet here it is."

Cass sat down across from her.

"Father said the green one told him we might not be worth the resources," Cass said. "Said they're asking about our use value."

His mother picked up another phone. Then another. She was gathering them now, pulling them toward her across the table. Collecting them.

"Your father is brilliant," she said. "And he's terrified. And he's already becoming something that isn't quite human anymore, just like you are, just like I'm starting to. But he's still thinking like a human. He thinks in terms of usefulness. Of demonstrating value."

She had all six phones now, arranged in a circle in front of her. She looked at them the way someone looks at their own bones.

"But your value isn't in usefulness," she said. "Your value is in what you are. What we are. We're the paradox that breaks their mathematics. We're the species that shouldn't be here, and we're still here, and we've built things that shouldn't be possible. The implant is starting to make you understand that. It's starting to let you see yourself from their perspective."

She met his eyes.

"And the fact that we can survive what they're trying to do to us? That's power. That's something they don't have. We're being remade, and we're still here. We haven't broken yet."

Cass felt the needle pulse. Three beats. Steady. The rhythm of something learning to belong to itself.

"What happens when the next group arrives?" he said.

His mother didn't answer right away. She was looking at the phones again, but not like they were tools anymore. Like they were evidence. Like they were the proof of something that needed to remain true even in the face of becoming something else.

"I don't know," she said. "But I know we're still ourselves. We're still human. And they're starting to understand that being human means something they don't have a category for yet."

She was wrong about that. Cass knew she was wrong. Because he could feel it now, in the place where the implant lived, in the frequencies that were starting to become readable to him—the crew didn't think of them as human anymore. They thought of them as something becoming. Something hybrid. Something that might eventually be useful in ways they hadn't predicted.

But he let his mother have this. He let her believe that what they were becoming was still tied to what they had been. Because maybe that was the only way to survive it.

He stood up and left her there with the phones. Walked out of the study, through the kitchen, toward the stairs that led up to the sleeping quarters. He could hear the hum of the bronze piece from down in the kiln, still resonating from where his father was working it. The sound had changed. It had a different character now that Cass had shown his father what it wanted to become.

At the top of the stairs, Cass paused. He could feel something moving through the water outside the house. Not just water. Intentionality. The crew was doing something. The collection apparatus was being activated. The final group was coming up.

He could hear it coming up.

Not with his ears. With the part of him that wasn't quite his ears anymore. The part that the implant had made possible. The part that could listen the way the ship listened, the way the crew listened—not to sound, but to displacement. To the moving of mass through the medium. To intention made manifest in pressure and temperature and the subtle redistribution of resources.

He went to his window. Opened it.

The city of squids was invisible in the darkness, but Cass could feel them. Moving. Preparing. The bronze piece sang its new frequency down in the kiln. His father was listening. His mother was holding the phones like talismans against a world that no longer had any use for them.

The needle behind his left eye pulsed three times and then it stopped.

When it started again, it would be at a different frequency.

Cass closed his eyes and waited for that moment to arrive.
