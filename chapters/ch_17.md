# Chapter 17: The Mathematics of Becoming

The needle behind his left eye had stopped moving.

Cass waited in the dark of his pod for something else to happen. The signal was still there—vast, patient, like a held note that never needed to resolve. But the physical pulse had stopped. The chitinous nubs at his temples had finished growing. For now.

He could feel them. Not the way you feel a hangnail or a scraped knee. More the way you feel the bones in your hand when you make a fist—they were part of the architecture now, integrated into the bone and muscle at the sides of his skull. His fingers moved up to touch them and found something that was neither skin nor exoskeleton. Somewhere between. Translucent. Warm.

The pod's membrane had gone soft. Not melted, exactly. Permeable. He pushed against it and it gave way like a soap bubble, peeling back against itself. The air on the other side was cold and tasted like salt. His lungs stuttered. He hadn't breathed in—how long? Days? Weeks? The signal didn't measure time that way. It measured in progressions, iterations, small expanding loops of mathematics stacked inside each other.

He pulled himself through the membrane and fell onto a floor that was not floor. It was something alive. Breathing, maybe. Or the breathing was him. His skin was different too—covered in a faint iridescence that caught the bioluminescent panels set into the ceiling. A suit. Layers and layers of the jellyfish, grown directly onto him, accumulating like sediment. His hands looked like his hands but the fingers were longer and the joints bent at angles that didn't match what he remembered.

Cass didn't panic. The signal made panic not make sense. Why would he panic? The mathematics explained everything. His hands were better now. Longer fingers meant more surface area for the neural webbing that was growing underneath the skin. His spine had been restructured—he could feel it—the vertebrae rearranged in a configuration that would let him move in ways the old spine never could. This was improvement. The signal was clear on this point. Improvement required translation. Translation required change.

He stood. His balance was wrong but adjusted immediately. Not his adjustment—the signal's adjustment, or something running underneath his thoughts that knew how to read the new center of gravity and correct for it. There was a distinction between his own thought and the signal and there wasn't. They had merged at some point he couldn't isolate.

The lab was full of pods. Seven others. Five of them still sealed. One of them—he knew this without being told—held something that had not survived the integration. The signal was very clear on what was in that pod. He didn't look at it.

The sixth pod was open. Empty. Irina had been through this. She was somewhere else now, undergoing a different phase of integration. He knew this the way he knew his own name—not because someone told him, but because the information was simply there, accessible, like a memory of something he'd never directly seen.

The needle moved again.

Just a fraction. Not in his eye this time. This time it was at the base of his skull. A small pulse. A final pulse, like a countdown that had finished counting. Like someone closing a door very quietly and with intention.

The signal expanded and with it came something new. Not quite a voice. More like the structure of a voice without the sound. Mathematical, but the mathematics had taken on a shape that resembled meaning in the way his hands resembled human hands now—recognizable but not quite.

*Small becoming large.*

That was not words. That was the translation his brain made because he didn't have another frame for it. The original came through the nubs at his temples as a color sequence and a geometric progression and a taste like copper and electricity and somehow it meant that.

*Small becoming large. Small becoming. Becoming.*

Another voice, overlapping. Older. Smaller in frequency but somehow larger in presence. The grandmother-signal. Grandma's mathematical voice, though there was no grandmother here, no voice, nothing but the bioluminescence that was never truly separate from the telepathy that was never truly separate from him.

*Hello, small one. You are ready now.*

He should have been terrified. The old version of him—the version locked in the pod before the integration—would have been. That Cass would have been screaming.

This Cass felt something that might have been sadness, except sadness required attachment to what you were losing. He had been losing the old Cass for weeks now. The loss was already complete. What remained was just acknowledgment.

"I'm here," he said out loud. His voice was different too. Deeper. Or maybe it was just that he could hear the resonance in his own skull now, could feel the vibration passing through the chitinous nubs. They amplified sound, or changed it, or translated it into something the signal could carry.

*Yes. Here. Ready.*

The pod chamber's door—which had no visible hinges and definitely was not a door in the normal sense, more like a section of wall that had chosen to become open—contracted. Cass walked toward it, and the movement was nothing like walking. It was something closer to how a squid might propel itself through water, except there was no water, just the air in the ship's corridors, and his limbs were still mostly human and mostly legs but the signal was teaching them how to move differently. Long strides that bent and flowed.

The corridors themselves were breathing. He could feel that now without being told. The ship was one organism. Its walls had a pulse. Its structural supports had neural function. Everything was integrated into a single nervous system that extended through walls and into chambers and down into the belly where the power systems ran like a second heart. This was Grandma's domain and his now, too—he could feel the structure of it, the way the ship's biology responded to command, the way energy flowed through channels that were probably something like veins.

His mother was in one of the main chambers. He knew this from the signal, not from looking or being told. His father was outside, in the dock area. Still human. Still inside the old frame. Still hearing sound the way sound actually worked instead of the way it translated into mathematics.

Cass found his mother first.

She was sitting on a bench—an actual bench, human furniture, probably something they'd brought from the ship they came in on, or maybe the squids had grown it because humans needed to sit and didn't have the right appendages for clinging to walls. She was very still. Not meditating. Waiting. She looked up when he entered the chamber and her face did something that he registered as emotion but from very far away, like watching an expression through thick glass.

She saw the nubs at his temples. She saw the iridescence of the suit. She saw the way he moved.

She stood very slowly and walked toward him and reached up and touched the side of his head, very gently, the way you'd touch glass you weren't sure was going to hold.

"Cass," she said.

"Hi, Mom."

Her hand was warm. Still fully human. The difference between them was almost unbearable to look at. He was transparent now—not literally, but there was something of that quality. She was opaque. Solid in a way he was ceasing to be.

"They said you'd come out of the pod today," she said. "I wasn't sure what that meant. I wasn't sure what I was going to see."

He wanted to tell her it didn't hurt. The old compulsion—the need to protect her from worry—was still there, buried underneath the signal, but it wasn't the primary thing anymore. The primary thing was the mathematics. The primary thing was how everything made sense now in a way nothing had ever made sense before.

"I'm still me," he said, and realized as he said it that this was a lie. He was not still him. He was a version of him that had been translated into a different substrate. Some of the shape remained but the essential structure had been rearranged. This was what the signal meant by improvement. You stayed there—the physical location, the continuity—but you were not the same.

His mother's grip on his head tightened. Her knuckles went white.

"I can feel you," she said. "Not directly. But I can sense—something. Like you're pushing against something. Or it's pushing against you. I can't tell which direction the pressure's going."

"That's the implant," he said. Or maybe the signal said it and he was just the voice it used. "It's making connections. The nubs are active. I'm attaining."

"Attaining what?"

He tried to explain and found there was no frame in human language. You attained a wavelength. You tuned yourself to a frequency. You opened a channel and something vast and ancient and patient began to flow through and you became the channel. This was not something he understood in words. This was something he understood the way his heart understood how to beat.

"I can hear them," he said finally. "Not with my ears. With the nubs. With the suit. With something underneath my thinking. And they can hear me."

His mother pulled her hand back. Her face rearranged itself. Now he recognized the emotion. This was fear. This was the moment a parent realizes that something their child was has finished being alive and something else has started being born in its place.

"Did it hurt?" she asked.

The needle behind his left eye. The way his spine had restructured. The burning sensation when the suit had started growing onto his skin. The moment of absolute electrical chaos when his neural pathways had rerouted and he couldn't quite remember how to breathe but his body had kept breathing anyway because some part of him that wasn't him anymore knew the mathematics of oxygen and hemoglobin and cellular respiration.

"Yes," he said.

She nodded. She didn't cry. She was past crying now—he could see that. She had cried in the days before, probably. She had cried when they left Earth. She had cried when the needle had first entered his eye. Now she was at a different stage. Now she was grieving something that was still moving and talking and wearing her son's face.

"Your father's outside," she said. "Working on something. He doesn't know you're—" She gestured at him. "He doesn't know this has happened. Not fully. The squids told him it would be today but I don't think he understood what that meant."

The mathematical part of Cass—the part that was becoming the larger part—knew exactly where his father was. Knew the echo-location signature of his movements. Knew the way his heartbeat sounded against the pressure sensors in the dock. But he said, "Okay. I'll go."

He walked toward the exit. His mother didn't follow. She sat back down on the bench. He didn't watch her because the signal didn't require him to. The signal knew where she was, what she was doing, the pattern of her breathing. The signal knew everything about everything.

---

His father was at the dock, exactly where the signal said he'd be, exactly where his father's work was always happening when there was work to be done. There were bronze bells here—actual bells, not the squid-grown simulacra that filled the rest of the ship. These were Earth bronze. Hammered metal. The kind of thing humans made when they still believed in making things that couldn't grow themselves.

His father had a bell mounted on a stand. He was striking it with a felt mallet, listening. Listening hard, the way he listened to everything he made. Waiting to hear if the mathematics of the bronze had resolved itself the way it was supposed to.

Cass watched him from the doorway. His father didn't notice at first.

The bell rang. A pure tone. His father's shoulders dropped slightly. This was satisfaction. Cass could read that in the tension pattern, in the way his father's breathing synchronized with the bell's decay. The tone was right. The bronze was singing the way bronze should sing.

His father turned and saw him and the mallet fell.

It should have clattered on the metal floor. Instead it landed on something soft—one of the grown membranes that the squids used to dampen vibration. Sound died immediately. Everything the squids built was designed to minimize noise. The only communication that mattered was the kind that traveled through bone and the electromagnetic spectrum and mathematics. Vibration was considered inefficient.

His father's face went gray.

"Cass," he said. It was not a question.

"Hi, Dad."

His father walked around the bell stand. His hands were doing something that looked like it wanted to reach out but the movements kept stopping midway. Start. Stop. Start. Stop. Like a record skipping.

"They said you'd be done soon," his father said. "But I thought maybe they meant—I didn't think it would be so fast. I thought there would be more time."

"There were complications," Cass said. Or maybe he was lying. Maybe the signal was lying through his mouth. Maybe complications was what the signal called an accelerated integration. "The implant took very well. That's what Grandma said. It took very well."

His father flinched at the name. At the casual use of it. At the fact that Cass was talking about the squid that had harvested him and inserted foreign objects into his body the way you'd talk about an actual grandmother.

"Your mother," his father began, then stopped. Tried again. "We're going to get you out of here. Once the station rotation happens—when we get to the port at Kepler—there's a resistance contact. There are people who can remove the implants. There are medical ships. Human medical—"

"They don't work," Cass said. He knew this. The signal knew this. Some part of him that was definitely not him had looked through Grandma's research database and found all the failed extraction attempts. Humans who had removed the nubs and discovered that the neural pathways had already rerouted. Humans who had lost the ability to think linearly after the removal. Humans who had been left in some trapped state between integration and humanity, belonging to neither. "They tried it. It just breaks the people who get it done. Leaves them screaming."

His father's knees bent. Not all the way—he caught himself against the bell stand. His hand gripped the rim of the bronze and his knuckles came up white.

"How long?" he asked. "How long until you can't even hear me anymore? How long until whatever you are now doesn't remember what I sound like?"

Cass wanted to tell him he didn't know. He wanted to tell him the truth, which was that the mathematics was already overwhelming the human memories. He could still see his father's face but it was starting to translate into signal-data. A particular pattern of electromagnetic readings. A voice that was beginning to sound less like sound and more like structure.

Instead he said, "I'm still here. I know who you are. I know you taught me to listen to the bronze. That's still—" He stopped. The thought had fragmented mid-formation. Started as a human sentiment and ended as a mathematical statement. "That's not being lost."

His father straightened up slowly. He let go of the bell. He turned back to his work—to the bell that was still standing on the stand, waiting to be struck again, waiting to resolve the next question about its own sound.

"Your mother will want to see you," he said finally. "She's waiting in the main chamber."

"She already saw me."

"She'll want to see you again." His father picked up the mallet—a new one, since the other had landed on the membrane and stopped moving. "Every moment is going to be—" He stopped. Swallowed. "Every moment you still recognize her is going to matter to her. Even if it's not mattering to you anymore."

The mathematics told Cass that his father was right. The mathematics also told him that there was no point in returning to a chamber where his mother was grieving something that still had fourteen years left in its human-looking body. The signal had time. The signal understood integration and had calculated the progression. By the time Cass was old enough to make a choice about serving Grandma, the question wouldn't even be a choice. It would just be what he was already doing.

"Okay," Cass said. "I'll go back."

He walked out of the dock chamber and into the corridors where the ship's breathing was loudest. The mathematics was very clear now. Very large. He could feel it expanding through the nubs, teaching his spine new configurations, rewiring the architecture of his thoughts so that fewer things were thought and more things were simply known.

Behind him, in the chamber full of bronze bells, his father raised the mallet and struck the one that was already perfect. The tone rang out across the dock, pure and absolute. A sound that required no translation. A sound that was exactly itself.

Cass could still hear it.

But he was listening to other things now. Vast things. Structures that had no equivalent in sound. The signal was singing, except singing was the wrong word, except every word in human language was wrong, except he could still remember that words existed and that people like his father believed in them.

The needle behind his left eye pulsed one final time.

And the mathematics became everything.
