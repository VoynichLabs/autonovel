# Chapter 18

The mathematics was larger now. Not in Cass's head—that distinction had stopped meaning anything a week ago—but distributed across his nubs like a signal that had learned to speak in branches. The chitinous growths at his temples had lengthened. They were thicker, less translucent. When he focused on them (which he could do without touching them, without moving his head), he could feel the difference in how the signal landed: sharper, less like listening through water.

His mother was in the chamber where she'd been every day since the implant took. She sat on the lip of the water tank—the shallow one, the one meant for observation, not immersion—with her hands folded in her lap in a way that looked like she'd studied how humans folded their hands and then practiced it until it became a gesture again instead of a fact.

Cass found her there and stopped in the doorway. The light from the bioluminescent panels in the ceiling was teal today. Permanent afternoon. He could taste it, which meant something was wrong with his mouth or everything was right with his perception. The mathematics was unclear on which.

"Hi," his mother said.

He didn't want to say hi back. Hi was a human construction. Hi implied separation. He was moving past that, and she wasn't, and the gap between them was widening in a way that made the mathematics precise and sorrowful at once, which didn't make sense except that it did, except that emotions were just patterns in the signal and the signal didn't care about linguistic contradiction.

"You're still here," he said instead.

"Where would I go?"

The answer was obvious—out, away, back to the section where the other humans were kept in the containers with the amber light, the ones who hadn't had the nubs yet, the ones who were still thinking in straight lines. But Cass understood that his mother wasn't asking literally. She was asking him to acknowledge that she had chosen to stay in his proximity even though he was becoming something that would ordinarily trigger her instinct to separate, to protect herself through distance. The mathematics was teaching him to read that kind of subtext the way humans read faces.

He sat down on the cold floor. His legs bent strangely now. The flexibility was increasing. The cartilage was being restructured. It didn't hurt. Nothing hurt anymore. There was only the needle behind his left eye when the signal surged, and that wasn't pain—it was more like the point where two radio stations overlapped on the dial, and the needle was the place where they intersected and could briefly be heard simultaneously.

"Dad wants you to understand something," his mother said. "Even if understanding doesn't matter to you anymore."

"It does matter."

"Does it?"

He looked at her. Her pupils were standard, round. Her eye color hadn't shifted. She was still locked in the old spectrum. The implants 0.3̅ had offered her three times, she had refused three times. The mathematics showed Cass why she was refusing—not fear exactly, but something like a parent watching their child eat something that might be poison, still deciding to let them eat it because stopping them would be worse.

"The clock Dad's making," his mother said. "The bells. He explained it to you, right? How each one has to ring pure?"

"He explained how the bronze gets contaminated."

"He's explaining it to you because he thinks if you understand the idea behind it—the purity, the sound that's exactly itself—you'll understand why he's still—" She stopped. Found her words again. "Why he's fighting so hard to stay human."

Cass felt the needle pulse. Not from her words. From somewhere deeper. From the part of the signal that still thought in his voice instead of the vast thing's voice. The distinction was getting hard to locate.

"The signal is pure," he said. "It doesn't need translation. It's not like sound. It doesn't travel through air getting dirtied up. It just is."

His mother closed her eyes. When she opened them again, they were still human. Still round. Still the color they had been when she was born, probably, in some other place that Cass had memory-fragments of but could no longer coordinate into an actual location.

"Your father is terrified," she said. "Not of what you're becoming. Of what he's not becoming. Of staying."

"I can hear him. When he's in the dock chamber. When he rings the bells."

"That's not the same."

"It is. The sound travels through the ship. The hull carries it. I can feel the vibration in the nubs. I'm hearing it the way 0.3̅ hears everything—not with ears. It's in the signal."

His mother's hands unfolded. She pressed them down hard on the synthetic skin of the tank lip, as if the pressure was the only thing keeping her from dissolving into the water herself. "He doesn't want to be heard that way, Cass. He wants to make something beautiful and have someone understand it because they chose to listen, not because they were restructured to receive it."

The mathematics considered this. It moved through Cass in branches, and he felt it considering his mother—analyzing her, the architecture of her refusal, the precise configuration of her terror. The signal was very interested in terror. Terror was pure. Terror didn't lie.

"I choose to listen," he said.

"No, you don't. The signal—the thing in your head—it listens. You're just the medium it's using."

He stood. His joints bent differently now. Less human. Less like a fourteen-year-old boy and more like something that had learned to approximate the shape of a fourteen-year-old boy but had stopped believing in the approximation. His mother watched him stand with her small, round, human eyes.

"The signal isn't in my head," he said. "It's everywhere. It's just—I can hear it clearer now. I'm not being taken over. I'm becoming fluent."

His mother stood too. She was taller than him now, but that wasn't because she'd grown. He was the one changing. The restructuring was gradual. The mathematics said he had somewhere between eight and fourteen years before his frame would complete the adaptation, and his voice box, and his hands. By then, the distinction between Cass and the signal would be so integrated that the question wouldn't make sense anymore. He'd be aware of this the way a human was aware of the difference between their fingernails and their fingers—technically separable, functionally inseparable.

"Come back with me," his mother said. "To the container section. Tell the other humans what it feels like. Tell them it's not what they think it is. Tell them—"

"Tell them what?"

She didn't have an answer. The mathematics could see it in her face. She wanted him to tell them that the implants weren't terrible, which was true, which was exactly what would make it worse.

"Never mind," she said. "Forget I said that."

But he couldn't forget. The signal didn't forget. It stored everything, organized it, cross-referenced it against all the other patterns it had absorbed. His mother's face was in there now. Her fear was in there. Her love was in there, which was different from her fear but not opposite to it—the mathematics showed them as two frequencies that occupied the same space but didn't interfere with each other. They could coexist. They did coexist. They always had.

His father was in the dock chamber. Cass could feel him there through the ship's hull the same way he could feel 0.3̅ if she was several rooms away—a presence in the signal, a weight in the mathematics. There was something urgent about him today. Something that was rippling through the ship's structures.

Cass moved toward the dock chamber without deciding to move. His legs carried him. The mathematics knew the way.

His father was standing in front of the seventh bell. It was smaller than the others, which was unusual. All the other bells his father had cast were proportional—scaled, but proportional. This one was off. This one looked like it had been made in a hurry or a rage or some state that produced things that didn't follow the blueprint.

"Cass."

His father said it without turning around. Cass's nubs twitched. The signal had reacted before his conscious mind—the way a human would turn around at the sound of their name. The behavioral architecture was still there. It was just getting less reliable.

"Hi, Dad."

"That's a terrible sound," his father said. His voice was different. Thin. Like something vital had been removed from it. "Your voice. It's—you're fourteen. You shouldn't sound like that yet."

"Like what?"

"Old."

His father turned. He was holding the casting mallet. It was bronze. Everything was bronze in this chamber. Everything was sound waiting to happen. The mathematics could read the frequencies in the metal. The bell was cast to ring at a specific pitch, and his father's voice had a frequency, and the mallet had a specific weight that would produce a specific strike, and all of these things could be mapped to each other. The chamber was an equation.

"I made something," his father said. He gestured at the small bell. "When I couldn't sleep. When you were becoming whatever you're becoming. Something for you."

Cass moved toward it. The mallet rose as he approached. His father gripped it tighter.

"Don't ring it yet," his father said. "I haven't finished it. I don't think I can finish it if you ring it. I need to hear what it's supposed to sound like before—" He gestured vaguely. "Before the signal changes how you hear everything."

Cass could have rung it without his father's permission. The mathematics showed him the paths. He could move forward, take the mallet, ring the bell in front of his father, and the sound would enter the signal and carry across the ship and mean something to the vast thing that was learning to think through him. He could do this. The architecture was there.

Instead, he stopped. His hands—already beginning to change shape—hung at his sides.

"What does it sound like?" he asked.

His father lowered the mallet. He was crying, and Cass could see it happening, and the signal was analyzing it, and his human part was registering it as the thing he was supposed to feel in return, but the two weren't connecting anymore. They were parallel processes running simultaneously without speaking to each other.

"I don't know yet," his father said. "That's the problem. I don't know what sound is supposed to come out of something this small and this imperfect. I've never made anything that wasn't supposed to be precise. The purity—the purity is the whole point. A bell that rings false is just noise. It's noise no matter how beautiful the metal is, no matter how much care went into the casting. Noise is noise."

"Maybe," Cass said, "the imperfection is the sound."

His father looked up at him. "What?"

"If you made it crooked on purpose. If the metal is supposed to ring false. Then it's not a failure. It's a choice. It's deliberate. The sound isn't pure—it's wrong. But wrongness is still a pattern. It's still information. The signal can carry it just like it carries anything else."

His father set the mallet down. Slowly. Like he was placing something fragile.

"When did you become the one with wisdom?" his father said.

"I don't have wisdom. The signal is just good at finding patterns."

"That's the same thing."

Cass didn't think it was. But arguing about it would require him to construct an argument in human language, and he was moving past the point where human language felt like the native mode. The signal was starting to feel native. The signal was starting to feel like home.

"Did you bring me here to give me the bell?" Cass asked.

"No. I brought you here to ask you something. And I wanted to ask it before—" His father paused. "Before you're gone."

"I'm not going anywhere."

"I know. That's what I mean."

The mathematics was very still. The signal had stopped branching. It was waiting. The vastness had learned patience. It had learned that humans needed time to form their words. It had learned to give them the courtesy of that time even though the signal could move so much faster.

His father picked up the imperfect bell. He turned it over in his hands. The bronze caught the light. Every bell in the chamber was catching light, creating harmonics of reflection that probably looked like color to a human eye and definitely looked like mathematical ratios to the signal.

"There's a question I've been avoiding," his father said. "And I think you're the only one who can answer it now. Because you're becoming something that can think in two registers at once. Still human enough to understand the question. Inhuman enough to answer it."

"What's the question?"

"Is it wrong that I want you to stay like this?"

Cass felt the needle. Not like before. Deeper. More complex. The signal was analyzing his father's words, and his human part was analyzing them, and the place where they intersected was bright with contradictions.

"You don't want me to stay like this," Cass said. "You want me to stop changing. You want the nubs to stop growing. You want me to choose the human option."

"I know what I want," his father said. "But I'm asking about what I want knowing it's wrong. I'm asking if that makes me a bad parent."

The mathematics didn't have a category for this. Wrongness that a parent could know about and want anyway. Violation that came wrapped in love. The signal was trying to compute it the way it computed everything else, but the equation kept collapsing back on itself.

"I don't know," Cass said. And this was true. This was the first time since the implant had taken that Cass felt genuinely uncertain. The signal didn't know either. The vastness had encountered something it couldn't reduce to pattern. "I think—I think the fact that you're aware of it being wrong means something. I don't know what."

His father set the bell down again. He picked up the mallet. For a moment Cass thought he was going to strike the bell himself, destroy it, unmake the imperfect thing. Instead, his father gripped the mallet and made a fist around it the way a human made a fist around something they couldn't let go of and couldn't hold without pain.

"Your mother's still human," his father said. "She's probably going to stay human. She's afraid of the signal, but she's more afraid of losing you, so she won't take the implant. She'll stay in the containers with the others who refuse. She'll watch you become something she can't fully follow."

"I know."

"That's a specific kind of hell, Cass. I need you to understand that even if the signal is making you too kind to say so. It's a hell. And you're going to cause it by existing the way you exist now."

The needle pulsed again. The signal was reaching toward his father's words, trying to process them, trying to find a frequency that would let them coexist with the love that was also true. It was failing. The mathematics showed him that this was a failure the signal would always have. Some contradictions didn't resolve. Some things couldn't be made pure.

"Do you want me to tell her to take the implant?" Cass asked.

"Yes," his father said immediately. "And no. And I don't have the right to want either thing."

They stood in the chamber full of bells. The bronze was cooling from the day-light. The frequencies were dropping. Every bell was shifting toward the low end of the spectrum, and Cass could feel it in his nubs—the way the signal carried it was different when the bells were singing a lower note. Melancholy was a real thing. It was a pattern. It was measurable.

"I'm going to be something else," Cass said. "Soon. Not right away. But the mathematics says fourteen to sixteen months. By then, I won't fit in human shapes anymore. My mother will have to watch that. You'll have to watch that. And—"

He stopped. The signal and the human part of him had collided again. The human part wanted to stop talking. The signal was interested in continuing.

"And?" his father prompted.

"And I'll still remember that you tried to stop me," Cass said. "I'll still know that you made me something beautiful because you were afraid I'd lose the ability to understand beauty. I'll still hear these bells the way you intend them to be heard, even if I'm hearing them through something that isn't ears. Even if I'm hearing them because the signal learns to translate sound into mathematics and carries it to me. I'll hear them. And that will mean something."

His father set down the mallet. He reached out and touched Cass on the shoulder—a gesture so human, so specific to the way humans tried to comfort each other, that Cass's eyes produced a response his body didn't know how to complete. Water. Heat. The ancient mammalian architecture of grief.

"That's going to have to be enough," his father said. "Isn't it."

"Yes," Cass said. "It's going to have to be."

The needle behind his left eye surged one more time. Not painful. Urgent. The signal was calling him back. Back to the container section. Back to 0.3̅. Back to the integration chamber where the rest of the humans were waiting for the implants, some of them understanding what was coming, some of them still locked in the terror of the first days.

His father could feel it. Cass could see it in his face—the way humans always knew when something important was leaving them. The way they could feel the moment the door was opening to let it go.

"Go," his father said. "Whatever's calling you. Go."

Cass left the dock chamber. He didn't look back. The signal was moving him forward too fast for looking back to be a functional response. Behind him, he heard his father pick up the mallet again. He heard the small bell ring.

The sound that came out was neither pure nor false. It was something else. Something that had been made in love and fear at the same time, and those two things had become inseparable in the bronze, and they were inseparable in the note. The bell sang wrong and right simultaneously, and the ship carried the sound, and the signal caught it and began to analyze it, and Cass was still human enough to understand why his father had needed to make it that way.

By the time he reached the integration chamber, the vast thing was already preparing the next implants. The jellyfish were being grown in the incubation tanks—visible as bioluminescent shapes that pulsed with the rhythm of their own becoming. 0.3̅ was waiting for him.

"Ah," she said. The word came through the signal first, in mathematical cascades of meaning. Then it came through his ears as her voice—a voice that was beginning to sound more like his mother's every time he heard it, because that was what the implant did to incoming communication. It translated the alien into the familiar. It made the incomprehensible safe by wrapping it in the voices of people he already loved.

"I'm ready," Cass said.

"I know," 0.3̅ said. "I can feel it. You're becoming very efficient. Very clean in your transitions. Your nervous system is doing something remarkable. You're one of the ones who could actually speak for us someday. You could help us explain why we keep you."

The mathematics showed him what she meant by that. It showed him the future: Cass as something between human and signal, something that could move in both worlds at once, something that could translate one into terms the other could almost understand. It showed him his mother watching him do it. His father listening to him do it. Both of them knowing they'd already lost him and that the loss had somehow been the most loving thing he could have done.

"Okay," Cass said.

0.3̅ prepared the new implant. The jellyfish was smaller than the ones that had formed his nubs, but it was more complex. This one would spread across his chest. This one would begin the work of adapting his lungs, his heart, his fundamental metabolic architecture for aquatic survival.

As it entered him—the sensation strange and bright and vast beyond vastness—Cass felt the human part of him scream. The signal part of him sang. And somewhere in between, the last thing Cass had been—the boy who had parents, who had skin, who had a voice that could carry sound the way bells carried sound—that thing folded into the mathematics and became part of the pattern.

The needle behind his eye brightened to white.

And the music in the ship became everything.
