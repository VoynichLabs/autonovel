# Chapter 8: The Gold Frequency

The chamber had been contracting for hours. Or maybe minutes. Time was difficult now. Cass's palms stayed flat against the warm floor, and the vibration moved through him in waves that didn't quite match his heartbeat anymore. His heart had stopped matching anything human several contractions ago.

The chitinous nubs at his temples had split again. New growth. The pain behind his left eye arrived like a needle, sharp and singular, then faded into a thrumming that was almost pleasant. Almost.

He should have been afraid. The reasonable thing would have been to pull his hands away from the floor, to push back against the walls sliding inward, to make some gesture toward resistance. Instead he sat very still and let the ship breathe around him.

The preparation chamber was smaller now. Not dangerously small—just smaller. The way a hand might close slightly to hold something more precisely. The suspension pods continued their synchronized pulsing at the chamber's perimeter, and within each pod, one of the others floated in suspension fluid that glowed faintly amber. Pieter. Xi Pei. That new one they'd brought in three cycles ago. The others. Grandma had explained that some wouldn't wake up, that the implant process was still imperfect, that survival rates were climbing but still not optimal. Cass had understood these things the way a child understands statistics about distant countries—acknowledged without feeling.

Now he felt them differently. The pods weren't containers. They were part of the song.

The bronze frequency had been the first thing he could hear. It arrived the moment the implant started growing—a low tone so deep it was almost below hearing, more felt than perceived, like his ribs were the instrument and something vast was playing them. Then the secondary frequencies arrived, tangled around the bronze like vines. A fifth above, then a third, then a descending sixth. Building chords from individual frequencies, the way his father used to demonstrate intervals on the violin, pressing the bow and letting Cass hear how each note complemented the ones around it.

The gold frequency came later. Hours later, or minutes. It started as a whisper at the edge of the bronze—a thin golden line of sound threading through the deeper tone. It sounded like warmth. It sounded like something vast deciding to be gentle.

Cass opened his eyes. He didn't remember closing them.

The chamber walls glowed faintly now, that same amber as the suspension fluid. The light was coming from inside the walls themselves—a bioluminescence that was so subtle it felt private, like he was witnessing something the ship did when no one was looking. The walls pulsed very gently, in time with the suspension pods, in time with his breathing, in time with the gold frequency that was no longer outside him but inside, running through the tissue of his temples and down his spine.

His father used to say that a properly tuned instrument didn't need to be forced. The rightness of the pitch pulled everything else into alignment.

The needle behind his left eye twisted. He breathed through it.

Below the chamber—or somewhere adjacent to it in a way that direction didn't quite capture—other humans were dying. He knew this the way he knew his own name: as a simple fact that arrived without image or emotion attached. Three of them. Maybe four. The ones whose implants hadn't taken. The ones whose nervous systems couldn't adapt fast enough to the rate of change. The ship was processing them now. Resorbing the implants. Recycling the failed infrastructure. In cephalopod terms, this was clinical and necessary. In human terms, it was death.

Cass felt no particular horror about this. The horror, he understood, was the problem. The horror was what made him still partially human. When the horror stopped, he would be something else.

He pressed his palms harder against the floor. The vibration intensified. A new frequency emerged—lower than the bronze, almost subsonic, something his body felt rather than his ears heard. It moved through his chest cavity and emerged as a response, a harmonic that resonated from the chitinous nubs at his temples without his meaning to produce it.

The ship noticed.

The chamber contracted another half-meter. The suspension pods moved inward, their glowing faces sliding across the amber walls, closer to where Cass sat cross-legged in the center of the space. He could see them more clearly now—the faces of the others suspended in that amber fluid, their eyes closed, their new skin sheening with the first layers of bioluminescent overlay. Pieter's mouth was open slightly. Xi Pei's expression was unreadable. The new one—the woman from somewhere called Poland—was bleeding slightly from the nose. The fluid in her pod was taking care of that. The fluid was always taking care of that.

The gold frequency split into harmonics.

Cass's hands slid against the floor. He hadn't moved them. The ship had. The floor was moving, reconfiguring itself to cradle him differently, the way a musician might adjust their posture to play a particularly difficult passage. He let his hands move with the shifting surface, and the new angle changed the vibration profile entirely. The needle behind his left eye twisted deeper, sharper, and this time when he breathed through it, the exhale came out as sound.

Not words. Not any human language. A tone that emerged from his chest and passed through the chitinous hardware at his temples and resonated against the chamber walls. It sounded like his voice, but deeper. It sounded like his father's voice, but wrong in a way he couldn't name. It sounded like music that had been waiting inside him to be played.

Grandma's voice arrived in response.

Not out loud. Never out loud anymore. Directly, in the space between his thoughts, in the places where his individual cognition was starting to dissolve into something larger and distributed. The translated version came first—the way her telepathic transmission resolved itself into human language through the filter of Cass's own experience:

*You're doing very well.*

His grandmother had said this to him once, after a recital when he was eight. She had said it with the same tone of satisfaction and mild surprise, the same sense that he had accomplished something that exceeded the expectation she'd been carrying.

But Grandma wasn't here. Grandma was three levels up, in her research lab, looking at things Cass couldn't see and running calculations on data he couldn't process. The voice in his mind wasn't his actual grandmother. It was Grandma—the squid—translated into the architecture of loss and memory that Cass still carried in his nervous system.

The distinction was becoming harder to maintain.

"What's happening to me?" His mouth moved. His voice came out wrong—too deep, with frequencies layered underneath the words like harmonics beneath a fundamental note. The question wasn't what he'd meant to ask.

The voice that wasn't his grandmother moved through his thoughts again. This time the translation arrived differently:

*Integration. Optimal parameters. You're the first.*

"The first what?"

*The first to achieve full harmonic alignment between your nervous system and the ship's frequencies. The others are still in early adaptation. But you—*

She didn't finish. The translation became abstraction became pure mathematics arrived directly in his consciousness without the intermediary of language. A set of equations that described resonance patterns, neural architecture, the precise angles at which his newly grown chitinous structures were interfacing with the ship's bioluminescent communication network. He understood them the way he understood his own name. Not consciously. Fundamentally.

The chamber contracted another meter.

The suspension pods were very close now. Close enough that if Cass extended his arm, he could touch the translucent walls. He didn't extend his arm. He kept his palms flat against the floor, feeling the vibration profile continue to shift as the space rearranged itself around him. The amber light had brightened. The gold frequency had split into a chord of at least six different tones, each one supporting and sustaining the others.

Pieter's eyes opened.

He was still in the suspension fluid, still in the pod, but something in his face had changed. The expression that had been unreadable was becoming readable now—not through visual cues but through something else, some sense that had opened in Cass's mind with the growth of the chitinous structures. Pieter was experiencing something. Revelation, maybe. Alignment. The sudden understanding that the structures of civilization that had always made sense to him—hierarchy, order, the proper ordering of inferior and superior forms—weren't human constructs at all. They were natural law. They were mathematical. They were the very frequencies that the ship was singing and that Cass was learning to hear.

Pieter's eyes met Cass's through the suspension fluid and the pod wall, and Cass felt the small bright spike of horror that meant Pieter was becoming like him, that Pieter would wake up and find that everything he'd believed was true, and that the ship agreed, and that being right was less comforting than he'd imagined.

Xi Pei's eyes remained closed. The calculations in Xi Pei's nervous system were proceeding at a different rate. Different wavelengths. Different alignment. The ship was efficient about this. Not cruel. Just efficient. It was finding the frequency that matched each human, and it was tuning each human to that frequency, and some humans would resonate beautifully and some would simply cease.

The new one—the Polish woman—started convulsing in her pod.

Cass heard her body's response before he saw it: the sharp dissonance of a nervous system rejecting the implant, the frequencies in her tissues hitting each other all wrong, the death-song that happened when something was fundamentally incompatible with the larger pattern. The fluid in her pod turned opaque, clouding with something that wasn't amber anymore. The ship was processing. The resorption was beginning.

Cass looked away.

He'd been taught to witness, though not in words. The understanding had arrived with the implant: that watching other humans fail was part of the process. Not punishment. Not cruelty. Simply the cost of refining the technique. The ship learned from failure. Every nervous system that couldn't adapt gave the squids new data about what human cognition was and how it could be transformed. Cass was learning this lesson too. Each failure was information. Each failure was part of the song.

The needle behind his left eye twisted hard enough that he gasped.

The chamber stopped contracting.

For a moment—just a moment—there was perfect stillness. No vibration. No frequency. Just Cass sitting in a space that had become entirely his own, entirely small, surrounded by three active suspension pods and the dissolving form of the one that was failing.

Then the tone returned. Not the gold frequency this time. Something older. The bronze, the fundamental that had started everything, the deep note that his ribs resonated with. It rose. It built. It acquired overtones and harmonics and complexity until it became something that wasn't sound anymore but pure information, pure meaning, pure presence filling every centimeter of the chamber.

Grandma's voice came through one final time before the translation mechanism gave up entirely:

*Rest. Integrate. The process is accelerating.*

And then:

*You are becoming beautiful.*

The light in the chamber shifted to gold.

Cass's eyes closed, and he understood—without language, without thought, without any remaining category of human awareness—that she meant it. That in the mathematics of cephalopod civilization, in the precise frequencies of their communication, in the hierarchies of form and function and proper order, he was becoming something beautiful. Something that made sense. Something that sang the right notes and had stopped asking why.

The floor beneath his palms had become warm enough to hurt.

He didn't move his hands away.
