# Chapter 6

The first thing Cass heard was bronze.

Not metaphorically. Not in the synesthetic way that had started after the implant—color arriving as taste, mathematics arriving as weight in his chest. Literal bronze. A B-flat struck on a tuning fork, the kind his father kept in the workshop for checking pitch on the larger instruments. The sound held for three seconds, then faded.

He opened his eyes.

The pod walls were translucent, milky white. Through them, shapes moved. Large shapes. Deliberate. The shadows passed back and forth—not erratic, not searching. Pattern. Routine. His heart did not accelerate. This surprised him. A month ago, waking to find himself inside something half-solid would have sent him into hyperventilation. Now it registered as information: he was in the preparation chamber. He was still in the pod. The shapes were the cephalopods. Nothing had changed except that he had been asleep and now he was not.

The chitinous nubs at his temples itched.

He reached up and touched one. Smooth, like the horn on a beetle's back. Warm. When he pressed it, the bronze sound returned—not from outside but from inside his skull. A resonance. His own nervous system, learning to sing at frequencies it had never been built for.

The pod membrane rippled. Softened. A current of warmer liquid pushed against his skin, and then the wall collapsed inward like something had taken a bite out of it. Cass climbed out without thinking about it. His legs held him. His body had learned, in whatever dreaming time had passed, how to move in this gravity with the extra weight of the implant sitting behind his eyes like a second brain trying to learn the shape of the first.

The preparation chamber was all curved surfaces and bioluminescent panels. The light came from the walls themselves—a soft amber that had no source point, just gradual everywhere-ness. No shadows. No corners. The cephalopod approach to architecture: grown, not built. Organic. Integrated.

0.3̅ was waiting.

She hung suspended in a tank on the far side of the chamber, her body draped through a section of water that was somehow held vertical, held in place by something Cass's implant was starting to recognize as mathematics but that his unenhanced eye could only see as an absence of floor. She had five of her eyes on him. The sixth was on the monitoring console that floated beside her—a jellyfish-like thing covered in smaller jellyfish, each one pulsing with different colored light.

When she saw him move, her whole body flashed green.

The signal hit before she spoke aloud—if what came out of her could be called speaking. Cass heard his grandmother's voice: warm, older, with the slight rasp of someone who'd smoked too long and was now past the point of caring about it. "You're awake," she said in English, the words arriving a half-second after the telepathic pulse had already told him the same thing, except the telepathic version had contained layers—pleasure and satisfaction and something else. Possession. The way you felt about a project that had turned out better than the research models suggested it would.

"How long?" His voice came out rough. His throat had been breathing water for a while, or something like water. His lungs tasted mineral.

"Thirty-two of your solar days. Your nervous system integrated faster than I predicted." The green pulses continued, almost lazy now. Satisfied. "You're my first human to attune across three simultaneous wavelengths. That shouldn't be possible. Your species' cognitive architecture isn't equipped for it. And yet."

Cass looked down at himself. The shimmer was gone—or rather, it had deepened. What had been a faint phosphorescence under the skin was now a layer, visible only when the light hit it at the right angle. Like oil on water. Like something that belonged to two places at once. His fingers looked almost normal until you tried to focus on them directly, and then your eye slid off and settled somewhere else.

"The three wavelengths," he said. "What are they?"

0.3̅ made a sound—a low rumble that vibrated through the chamber's walls. Her communication organ, Cass realized. The same way the walls transmitted their own speech. She was using all of her tools at once. "Navigational. Biological. And something I don't have a category for yet. You're picking up fragments of conversations that don't involve you, and you're not being flooded. Your brain is filtering. Most humans don't. Most humans catch one wavelength, maybe two, and then they start to come apart."

"And the ones who don't survive?"

"I collect them after." She pulled one eye away from the console and looked directly at him. "Their implants don't reject their biology. Their biology rejects the implants. The incompatibility is cellular. I dissect them. I learn what went wrong. That knowledge helps the next batch."

She said this the way a musician might describe how to recover from a bad recording session. Casual. Focused. A thing that happened in the course of doing the work. Cass felt something move in his chest—not quite recognition, not quite horror. Something in between. Something his grandmother's voice was saying with obvious affection didn't actually make it gentler.

"I need you to do something for me," 0.3̅ continued. She released whatever force was holding her suspended, and she sank gracefully into her tank, the water rising to accommodate her body. "Come here."

He walked across the chamber. The floor was warm. The air had weight to it—thick, humid, full of the smell of something organic and vaguely fermented. The smell you encountered in compost bins or the back of a reef tank when the filtration stopped working. Not foul. Just overly alive.

The monitoring console hung at the level of his chest. Up close, Cass could see that the jellyfishes making up the device were actually doing something. They pulsed in sequence, each color holding information that his implant was barely translating—fragments of meaning that arrived as sensation instead of knowledge. Pressure. Temperature. A sense of other heartbeats in the lab. The humans in the other pods, still dreaming, still dreaming their way toward integration or toward dissection.

"Put your hand on it," 0.3̅ said.

He did.

The moment his skin made contact with the gelatinous surface, information flooded in sideways. Not like words arriving in his head. Like opening a book and having all the pages suddenly exist at once, and his mind having to unfold itself fast enough to parse the structure. He saw the other humans from the outside—their nervous systems rendered as probability maps, their integration rates displayed as slow curves rising from left to right. Xi Pei was tracking at forty-two percent integration. Irina was at thirty-nine percent. The Afrikaner—Pieter—was climbing faster than the other early subjects, his curve rising at a steep angle, his attunement pattern showing a concentration in administrative wavelengths that 0.3̅'s notes flagged as "anomalous but internally coherent."

And there were others.

Cass saw the rows of suspended pods behind 0.3̅'s private section, the ones he'd never been brought to see. The ones she called her collection. Seventeen more humans. Twenty-three cephalopods of smaller subspecies, their bodies dark and dormant. A humanoid of indeterminate species with skin that looked like it had been grafted from three different bodies. Something that had too many joints and had stopped integrating weeks ago, its body halfway between two forms, trapped in the space between states.

His hand jerked away from the console.

"You'll get used to it," 0.3̅ said. Her voice was kind. That was the worst part. She wasn't cruel. She was simply describing her work, the same way you might describe tending a garden. "The sensory overload decreases as your implant matures. Your brain will learn to filter. To ask for specific information rather than receiving everything broadcast. Right now you're catching all of it because you're still learning the language."

"Why did you show me?"

"Because you need to understand your context. You're not unique. You're not even the most complex integration I've achieved. But you are the fastest, and the fastest integrations usually mean—" She paused. Her eye rippled, a color cycling through the spectrum so fast Cass couldn't track it. When she spoke again, his grandmother's voice had an edge. "—the fastest integrations usually suggest something unusual about the baseline architecture. Your mind was already half-adapted for this. I want to know why."

Cass found himself speaking before he'd chosen to. "My father made instruments. Brass mostly. Bells sometimes. You work with sound long enough, you learn to hear things that aren't quite there yet. The harmonic that's going to resonate if you adjust the temperature, the shape, the thickness of the wall. You learn to live in multiple frequencies at once, hold them in your head as possibility. You learn to build the space inside your skull the same way you build a resonance chamber—angles, proportions, tolerances. Maybe the implant found a chamber that was already built to specification."

The moment it was out, Cass regretted it. He'd just told her the thing that made him useful. The thing she'd been trying to understand. The thing that would keep him breathing but not free.

0.3̅ made that rumbling sound again, but this time it was different. Faster. Shorter. A punctuation rather than a sustained note. Cass had learned enough of her communication to recognize it as the sound of satisfaction—the pleased rumble you made when a problem solved itself and saved you hours of research.

"Yes," she said. "Yes, that explains the navigational attunement. Sound travels through medium. Water is your father's other domain implicitly—resonance tanks, pitch correction, all of it dependent on how sound moves through volume. You were already thinking like a cephalopod without knowing it."

The implication arrived before the full thought did. Cass felt the weight of it settle into the space behind his eyes where the implant lived. He had been marked for this his entire life. The things that had seemed like his father's obsessions, his peculiar way of moving through the world, had been training. Preparation. Adaptation for a transformation he was supposed to have died trying to resist.

"I need you to come with me," 0.3̅ said. She was already sliding herself out of the tank, her arms wrapping around the edge with boneless grace. "There's someone who wants to meet you."

---

The corridors of the ship were grown, not hallways in any conventional sense. They were esophageal—fleshy, faintly warm, with the sensation of being peristalsis'd forward rather than walking. Cass found that if he didn't think about it too carefully, his balance adjusted fine. Think about the movement and he'd start swallowing wrong, start feeling like the ship was digesting him, which was close enough to technically accurate that he tried not to pursue the thought.

0.3̅ moved ahead of him with the ease of someone in her own home, her lower arms trailing along the walls, reading whatever information was being transmitted through the living tissue. Cass caught fragments of what she was perceiving—the biological status of the ship itself, the integrity of various systems, the fact that the amphibious-caste crewmate was currently in the secondary lab doing something 0.3̅'s wavelength-reading indicated as "unnecessary." She flickered a pulse of communication down through the wall, and somewhere deeper in the ship, Cass sensed a response—the sense of being scolded, and the immediate cessation of whatever the crewmate had been doing.

Even the hierarchy moved through water and walls here.

They emerged into a chamber that was larger than the preparation room but organized the same way—curved, organic, lit from within. This one had pools built into its floor, not suspended in defiance of gravity but sunk, the water dark and deep. Around the pools, observation stations had grown from the walls—jellyfishes of various sizes, all pulsing with their independent logic, all feeding data into the larger network.

And suspended in the largest pool, alone, was a human.

Not one of the abductees. Something else. Something that had been human and was still partially human but had committed too far to the transformation to be either thing anymore. The body was tattooed with bioluminescent patterns that flickered in slow, dying waves. The arms had been reshapen—fingers fused into something longer, more flexible. The face was the worst part: still recognizably human in its structure, but rendered in water, floating, the eyes open and unfocused, the expression caught somewhere between peace and agony.

"This is what happens when someone refuses the integration," 0.3̅ said gently. "When they fight, when they reject the implant, when their nervous system tries to return to baseline human function while the implant keeps pushing for transformation." She gestured toward the suspended form with one arm. "The body tries to be two things. Eventually it becomes neither. Death takes six to eight of your weeks this way."

"Who was it?"

"Another researcher. From the Amphibious caste. Became enamored with the idea of experiencing human consciousness directly. Requested permission to undergo the implantation process." 0.3̅'s colors shifted—deep blues and blacks now, something that Cass's implant translated as something like sorrow. "Died proving that the concept of choice doesn't map well across species barriers. You can't choose something you have no framework to imagine choosing."

Cass forced himself to keep looking at the body. Some part of him understood that this was another form of teaching. Not cruel teaching. Necessary teaching. The kind where you showed someone the thing that would happen if they made the wrong choice, and you did it while you were being kind about it, while you were genuinely sad about the necessity.

"Why did you bring me here?" he asked.

"Because Delta Del wanted to meet the human who attuned across three wavelengths. Because he doubts that you're actually integrated and not simply an elaborate simulation of integration—your body mimicking the responses without the underlying cognition actually shifting. And because I wanted him to see that you're still human enough to understand what that body means, but integrated enough that the understanding doesn't paralyze you." 0.3̅ turned to look at him directly. "You're not paralyzed, are you?"

Cass examined himself. The shock was there—real, visceral. But it wasn't freezing him. He could still think. Still move. The implant was filtering it, making it into data rather than trauma. His father would have recognized this as a fundamental shift in how his mind processed sensation. The implant wasn't just adding neural pathways. It was rebuilding the architecture that determined what was signal and what was noise.

"No," he said. "I'm not."

"Good." A pulse of green flashed down through the wall, and a section of it rippled open. Delta Del Squared emerged first—orange-red bioluminescence, larger than 0.3̅, with the kind of aggressive posture that made clear this was a being who'd never second-guessed its place in a hierarchy. Behind him came the third crewmate, the energy specialist, trailing her bright green patterns like a wake.

Delta Del Squared's eyes swept over Cass with the kind of assessment that predators used. Cass felt the data coming through his implant—the cephalopod reading his neural activity, his integration markers, his atunement breadth, all of it displayed for analysis.

"You're right," Delta Del Squared said, his colors shifting through a sequence Cass's implant recognized as skepticism. "He's integrated. But there's something you're missing, 0.3̅."

"What?" Her green pattern flickered with the first note of defensive coloration.

"Look at his attunement to the administrative wavelength. That shouldn't be there at all. His cognitive architecture is acoustic, navigational, biological. Why is he picking up logistics and hierarchical data?" Delta Del Squared moved closer, his large body creating the kind of shadow in the room's light that made Cass's skin respond with small atavistic contractions. "Where did that come from?"

Cass heard himself answering before the question had fully registered. "My father kept records. Books. Data written down, physical, stored. The way you organize information if you can't broadcast it. The way you catalog if you have to remember by hand. And he built for clients—nobles, mostly, people with specific requirements, specific status. You learn how those people think. How they organize their wants and what they value. You learn the architecture of how power works when you're building something someone powerful is going to use every day."

The administrative wavelength, Cass was starting to understand, was the language of relationship and hierarchy. Of who could tell whom to do what, and why those relationships held their shape even when questioned. His father had spent his entire life building for the nobility—had spent his entire life learning how to anticipate what they wanted before they knew themselves. That was an administrative wavelength skill. The implant had simply given him access to the native language for what he'd always known how to do.

Delta Del Squared's colors cycled through something that might have been respect. "Interesting. You're not just adapted. You're adapted in ways that your species hasn't consciously realized it was being adapted."

"What does that mean?" Cass asked.

"It means," 0.3̅ said, her green flashing brighter, "that your entire species might be more suited to cephalopod civilization than we've been assuming. Which means my research has implications that extend far beyond this single crew. Which means—"

She didn't finish. An alarm rippled through the walls—a pulse so sharp and insistent that Cass felt his nervous system want to run before his consciousness caught up to what was happening. The alarms were inside the structure of the ship itself. This was its language, screaming.

Delta Del Squared's colors went hard orange. "Hull breach. Starboard secondary chamber."

"Impossible," 0.3̅ said, but she was already moving, her arms wrapping around the communication jellies, her whole body focused on interfacing with the ship's status. "I maintained integrity protocols. We're not in disputed space. We're not under attack."

"Then you have a structural failure," the green-pattern specialist said. "One of the collection tanks. Probably the one with the non-integrated humanoid. Its body was degrading. It could have ruptured."

They moved as one—a coordinated unit, the kind of thing Cass's implant was starting to recognize as the basic function of crew. Specialized roles, split attention, everything flowing through the administrative wavelength. He found himself moving with them, moving before they asked him to, understanding what needed to happen the way you understood a piece of music your father had taught you to play.

"Not you," 0.3̅ said, catching him with one arm as they moved toward the chamber exit. "You're not sealed. You can't survive depressurization."

"But I need to—"

"No." Her grip was gentle but absolute. "You need to go back to your pod. Now. If the breach is as severe as the alarm suggests, we're going to have to emergency seal the preparation chamber to protect the integration tanks. I can't risk you. You're integrated. You're valuable. You're safe."

He watched her move away from him, watched her flow through the corridor with Delta Del Squared in formation, watched her move the way she moved in water but in air, in the strange medium of the ship's interior. Watched her disappear into an emergency he couldn't process, couldn't help with.

And in the space where she had been, Cass heard bronze again—the same B-flat that had woken him in the pod. The implant singing. His nervous system resonating with the frequency of the ship's distress, learning to hear the thing underneath the alarm.

Learning to hear the music of disaster.

He started walking back toward the preparation chamber without waiting to be told. The walls were closing already—the ship was sealing itself, compartmentalizing, using the living tissue of its architecture the way his father used wood to shape sound. Separate the resonance chambers. Isolate the frequencies. Don't let the chaos of one section destroy the integrity of the whole.

By the time Cass reached the preparation chamber, the emergency door had sealed behind him with a sound like a throat closing. He was alone with six humans in suspension, alone with the children of the experiment, alone with the understanding that he was no longer quite human enough to be one of them but not yet alien enough to stand with the crew that ran the ship.

Alone in the space between frequencies, listening to the bronze sound continue to resonate against bone.

Listening to the mathematics of his own transformation echo back at him from every wall.
