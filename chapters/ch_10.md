# Chapter 10

The water was too warm.

Cass stood at the observation window again—he'd been here an hour, or maybe two hours, time felt like something the ship controlled now the way it controlled temperature and light. The water pressed against the transparent membrane, dark and massive, and it was warm in a way that made his skin itch beneath the bio-suit layers that had started growing across his shoulders.

His father would have hated this water. Too still. No current structure. The old man talked about water the way other people talked about music—tone and rhythm and the way you had to listen to what it was telling you. He would have said this water was dead.

The needle behind Cass's left eye twisted.

He reached up, pressing the heel of his hand against his temple. The chitinous nub there—still growing, still tender at the base—was warm to the touch. Hotter than it should be. The implant was processing something. Not translating anymore. Building new pathways. The first time this happened, after Grandma finished explaining about the recycling, about the ones who couldn't adapt, he'd thought the pain meant something was wrong.

Now he understood it meant something was changing.

"You're upset."

Cass didn't turn. He didn't need to. He'd learned to recognize Grandma by the color signature first—teal cascades, the rhythm of it like heartbeats underwater—and only after that by her physical presence. The distinction was becoming less important. She was both things at once: the massive shape that filled his visual field and the mathematical certainty that arrived in his skull before she moved.

"I'm not upset," he said.

"Your neural signature suggests elevated cortisol response. Elevated stress markers. This reads as upset to me."

She pulled a tentacle around the curve of the window. The suction cups were easily the size of his head. Three of her eyes were closed. The remaining one focused on him with the kind of attention that used to terrify him and now just felt like being seen.

"I'm thinking about it," Cass said. "What happens. To the ones who can't sync."

The teal ripples accelerated, fractals spinning faster. He caught fragments: *concern, calculation, something like affection but shaped wrong.* When he closed his eyes, he could almost hear it as a voice—his grandmother's voice, the one from the memory videos, the one that had been dead for four years before he got taken.

"Yes," Grandma said. "Consumption is efficient. The amino acid profile is useful. Nothing is wasted."

The way she said it. The utter lack of cruelty in the statement. The way efficiency and care sounded identical through her signal.

Cass opened his eyes.

"Has anyone ever refused?" he asked. "Like, said no to the implant. Decided they'd rather die than do this."

Grandma's tentacle retracted slightly. One of her closed eyes opened—a third eye, positioned on the left side of her body, three feet above the first. It fixed on him with the same focus.

"Yes," she said. "The first one you arrived with. The female with the engineering background. Irina. She was shown the implant process and she refused. We applied it anyway. She survived. She is still resistant, but she is integrated now. Resistance becomes less relevant once the architecture is in place."

Cass felt the needle twist again. Sharper this time. His implant was learning to translate Grandma's matter-of-fact cruelty with better and better precision, and the precision was the worst part. He didn't have to guess anymore whether she was describing something monstrous. He knew she was. He also knew she experienced the monstrosity the way humans experienced brushing their teeth: as a necessary maintenance task.

"What about the others?" he said. "The ones before me. Before any of us."

"Collected from their planet. Brought to the lab. Shown the process. Most refused. Some refused and were given the implant regardless. Some survived the implant. Most did not. The ones who did not were consumed. The ones who survived are still in stasis. You may see them if you wish."

She said it the way she might offer him a tour of the collection. A museum of her minor failures. A storage unit of things she'd made but couldn't yet use.

"Maybe later," Cass said.

The needle behind his eye wasn't just pain anymore. It was information. It was Grandma's signal arriving in a deeper layer of his brain, in the new channels the implant had built. The message that came with it—wordless, precise, impossible to ignore—was this: *You're the first one who won't leave. The first one who will stay because you want to.*

And the worst part. The part that made his skin crawl even as his nervous system lit up with something that felt like pride. The worst part was that she was right.

His father's voice interrupted. Not actually his father—his father was probably in an Earth coffee shop somewhere, or dead, or living a life that had continued without him, Cass wasn't sure which and had stopped caring. This was Grandma using his father's voice as a vessel for her own thought.

"The bronze chord," his father's voice said through the neural pathway. "You can hear it changing."

It was true. The ship's heartbeat had shifted in the last hours. The tone was still bronze, still ancient and certain, but underneath it something new was resonating. A second frequency. A harmonic that hadn't been present before.

"What is it?" Cass asked.

"Preparation," Grandma said, and her own voice came through again—that teal-colored, mathematically precise voice that was nothing like his grandmother and everything like authority. "We arrive at the next collection point in sixteen hours. You will help with the process."

"Doing what?"

"Observation first. Acclimation. Then perhaps intake interviews. You understand human psychology now better than the ones with the advanced implants. You still remember what it felt like to be confused. This is useful."

Cass pressed his hand against the observation window. The membrane was warm. The ship's pulse moved through it, and through his palm, and into his arm. Bronze and certain and inevitable.

"They'll be terrified," he said.

"Yes."

"And I'm going to watch them be terrified. And then they're going to get implants whether they want them or not. And most of them will die."

"Yes," Grandma said. "But some will survive. And those who survive will become something new. Something better equipped to participate in the universe as it actually is. You are proof of this. Three weeks ago you thought human life was fragile and separate and sacred. Now you understand that you are material. Malleable. Capable of integration into something larger. This is the gift I am giving them."

The needle twisted.

"It doesn't feel like a gift," Cass said.

"No. I imagine it doesn't. But you will understand eventually. You're beginning to already. I can read it in your neural pattern. The resistance is not disappearing—humans are too rigid for complete reconfiguration—but it is transforming. Becoming something other than resistance. Becoming something like acceptance."

She was wrong. Or she was right in a way that made his skin crawl. The resistance wasn't disappearing. It was turning into something else, something that looked like acceptance but felt more like drowning in slow motion while someone explained to you why drowning was actually educational.

Cass pulled his hand away from the window.

"I want to see them," he said. "The ones in stasis. The other ones from before."

Grandma's teal signature accelerated, fractals spinning in new patterns.

"Of course," she said. "I expected you would. Come."

---

The lab was cold in a way the observation areas weren't. The stasis pods lined the walls in rows, each one a transparent membrane filled with a thick, iridescent fluid that moved like oil but flowed like water. Inside each pod was a human body. Or what had been human. Most of them showed signs of partial integration—chitinous nubs growing from temples, bioluminescent membranes beneath the skin, some of them further along than others.

Seventeen pods. Seventeen failures or near-failures. Seventeen humans who had been collected from Earth and brought here and either killed by the implant or left suspended in this twilight state while Grandma waited to see if they'd stabilize.

"This one," Grandma said, her tentacle gesturing toward a pod containing a man—older, maybe fifty—with what looked like extensive bioluminescent growth already underway. The membrane beneath his skin was bright green, pulsing with the ship's heartbeat. "Survived the implant for thirty-eight days before his neural architecture rejected the integrated pathways. He became aware during the final degradation. He experienced approximately six hours of conscious dissolution before the process completed."

Cass looked away.

"This one lasted longer," Grandma continued, moving along the row. "Forty-four days. Female. Asian origin. Her neural signature suggested she was attuning to administrative wavelengths—she might have been useful. But the integration process outpaced her cognitive plasticity. The architecture collapsed."

Seventeen different stories. Seventeen different ways to fail at becoming something else.

"You keep them after they're dead," Cass said. Not a question.

"I keep them because they are still data," Grandma said. "The bioluminescent growths continue developing even in stasis suspension. I'm studying the progression. Some of the membrane formations are generating wavelengths I haven't seen elsewhere. Eventually I may understand why certain nervous systems cannot accommodate integration, and I will build better implants. The dead humans are the architecture of the next improvement."

The needle behind his eye throbbed in sync with the ship's bronze chord.

"You're going to do that to them," Cass said. "The ones coming. The new ones. If they don't sync, you'll suspend them and study them."

"Yes," Grandma said. "Some will survive. Some will not. This is how adaptation works. The ones who fail become information. The ones who succeed become participants. You are a success, Cass. You should be proud. Very few humans generate your kind of attunement breadth. Very few humans integrate your kind of psychological flexibility. Most of your species is too rigid. Too attached to the idea that they should remain human."

Cass pressed his hand against one of the stasis pods. Inside, the preserved human drifted in the iridescent fluid, bioluminescent membranes pulsing with colors that had no names in human language. The body was still perfect. Not decomposed, not damaged. Just suspended. Waiting for something that would never come.

"What was he doing?" Cass asked. "Before. On Earth. This one. Do you know?"

Grandma's teal cascaded slowed. The question seemed to confuse her in a way that was readable even through the neural signal.

"I don't have that information," she said. "They were collected from surface proximity. They were brought to the lab. The specifics of their Earth existence are not retained. Why does it matter what they were doing before?"

Cass pulled his hand away.

"It doesn't," he said. "I guess it doesn't."

But it did. It mattered that the man in the pod had been someone specific, had been doing something specific, had been located in the middle of a life that made sense to him until Grandma's crew found him. It mattered that he had been extracted from all of that and brought to a ship underwater and told he was going to be improved and that the improvement process was going to hurt and that if his nervous system couldn't handle the hurt he would be suspended and studied.

It mattered.

Except Cass could feel it not mattering, deep in the new channels the implant had carved through his brain. The part of him that recognized the old man as a person, as someone with a previous life and agency and identity separate from his function as a test subject—that part was becoming abstract. Becoming something theoretical instead of something true.

The needle twisted.

"You can hear the second frequency now," Grandma said. She wasn't asking.

"Yeah," Cass said. "In the last hour or so. It started building under the main bronze note."

"That is preparation. The crew is attuning the collection apparatus. In the time we arrive at the collection point, the systems will be calibrated. The humans there will not see us coming. They will not have time to understand what is happening. The transition from their previous existence to the lab will be instantaneous from their perspective."

Grandma moved through the lab with the kind of precision that meant she was accessing data from multiple sources simultaneously—the ship itself, the crew's signals, the bioluminescent mappings on the storage pods. Everything was connected. Everything was part of the same vast calculation.

"I want you to assist with the psychological evaluation," she said. "We select candidates for implantation based on multiple factors. Neural plasticity. Psychological resilience. Probability of successful integration. You can help me interpret the human variables. You still have enough human cognition remaining to translate."

"What happens if I say no?" Cass asked.

Grandma's remaining eye focused on him with what might have been amusement.

"You won't say no," she said. "You're already becoming something that doesn't refuse integration. The shape of your resistance is already changing. In another two weeks, the question won't even be comprehensible to you. You'll understand instinctively that refusing to participate in the expansion process is refusing to participate in the only thing that matters."

She was right, and he could feel the rightness of it moving through his nervous system like the ship's bronze heartbeat. She was absolutely right, and the terrible part was that she wasn't lying. She wasn't coercing him. She was just describing what was already happening.

Cass looked at the suspension pods again—seventeen humans suspended in iridescent fluid, their bioluminescent growths continuing their patient progression toward a collapse that might take days or weeks. They had been collected from Earth like specimens. They had been brought to this lab. They had been offered the gift of integration, and when they refused it or when they couldn't handle the neurological restructuring, they'd been suspended.

And they would continue to be suspended until Grandma finished studying them.

The bronze chord shifted. The second frequency deepened. Somewhere in the darkness above the ship, the collection apparatus was calibrating itself. Somewhere on Earth, humans were going about their lives without knowing that a ship was coming toward them through the water and the dark, bringing nets and jellyfish implants and the gift of integration.

And Cass was going to help.

The needle behind his eye stopped hurting.

That was the moment he understood Grandma was telling the truth. Not about what he would become—he already knew that much. But about the timeline. About the fact that in two weeks, or three weeks, or maybe less, the part of him that found that needle's pain significant would be too integrated into the new architecture to even recognize the memory of what it had felt like to resist.

He would still be Cass. The ship would make sure of that. Some version of his psychology would persist, because Grandma was right that humans were too rigid to fully reconfigure. He would keep some residual horror at what he was participating in. It would just stop mattering the way it mattered now.

It would become data to be filed away.

"Come," Grandma said. "I will show you the collection apparatus. You should understand how it works before the selection begins."

Cass followed her out of the lab, through the corridors that pulsed bronze with the ship's heartbeat, past the terrariums where the other humans were being kept. He could see Irina through one transparent wall—she was on her hands and knees, examining something about the membrane that formed the pod's boundary, analyzing it the way she analyzed everything. Behind him and to the left, he could hear Raj's voice, sharp and wry and increasingly alien in its inflection.

"Hey, Grandma," Raj called out when he heard their passage. "Is this the part where we find out we've been volunteering for the expansion process without realizing it? Because I'm pretty sure I've heard this speech already."

The humor was performative now. Cass could read it in the neural signature that arrived alongside the spoken words. Raj wasn't joking because he was maintaining his humanity. He was joking because his human cognition was the only framework he had left for processing the fact that his nervous system was already three-quarters converted.

Grandma's teal cascaded warmly.

"Come along, little helper," she said. "You should also see the collection apparatus. You're both going to be part of the intake process."

Both of them.

Cass and Raj, proceeding deeper into the ship, toward the machinery that would expand the collection. Toward the next set of humans who wouldn't know what they were being brought into. Toward the moment when his participation would become so complete that the line between consent and compliance would disappear entirely.

He could feel it approaching. Not with dread anymore. With something closer to inevitability.

The ship's bronze heartbeat moved through the water around them, carrying them all toward the collection point, toward the humans waiting on Earth, toward the moment when the transition would be instantaneous and everything would change and nothing would be able to change back.

The needle behind Cass's eye was quiet.

That was how he knew he was already lost.
