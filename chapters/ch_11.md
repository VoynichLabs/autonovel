# Chapter 11

The ship's bronze heartbeat had changed.

Cass heard it first in the pipes that ran through his quarters—a stuttering rhythm, quicker than before, with a skip in the middle that made his teeth ache. He lay in the narrow pod-bed that had been grown for him (soft, pulsing, somehow warm) and listened to the sound propagate through the hull plating. The acoustic signature was wrong. Not threatening, exactly. Just different. Off.

He pulled himself out of the pod and went to the porthole.

Outside, the water was darker than usual. A brownish-blue instead of the clean teal that normally filtered through. Something was filtering it—particles in the water, or blood, or something organic that hadn't been there yesterday. The current against the hull was faster. He could hear that too: the pressure of acceleration, the grinding howl of the ship's spine stretching and contracting as it changed vector.

They were moving. Grandma had been moving for hours, maybe longer. Cass tried to remember what time it was—tried to remember if time meant anything in the way it used to—and found the question dissolving before he could finish thinking it.

The needle behind his left eye pulsed. Not with pain, not exactly. With presence. Like something foreign and alive, taking up space in his skull where space shouldn't have been. The chitinous nub that had grown from his temple was sensitive now. Raw. When he touched it with his fingertip, a cascade of color flooded his visual field—a kind of taste-flash, geometric and hostile.

He pulled his hand away.

The intercom crackled. Not Grandma's voice—hers came directly now, threading into his skull like muscle memory—but the ship's external communication system. Mechanical. Primitive. A concession to the human crew members who still needed it.

"All personnel to the collection bay," Irina said. Her Russian was thick, coating the words like ice. "Now."

Cass dressed in the suit they'd grown for him. It was no longer strictly a suit—the distinction had blurred. The initial jellyfish implant was now layered with six additional bio-membrane coverings, each one grown as his integration progressed. They'd started as separate pieces, slipping on like a second skin. By the fourth layer, they'd stopped separating. By the sixth, Cass could no longer tell where the membrane ended and his own skin began. There was a pressure there, a steady hum that made his thoughts slow and deliberate.

The suit was teal with streaks of black.

The color signature of something in transition.

---

The collection bay was a cathedral of flesh and bone—a space carved directly from the ship's interior, the walls still wet, still breathing in that slow, patient rhythm that Cass had come to recognize as the ship's actual heartbeat, distinct from the percussion that moved through the pipes. The air was dense with salt and something else, something organic that coated his implant antennae with a texture like clay.

Irina stood at the center of the bay, her suit hanging loose on her frame. She'd lost weight. The implant process required calories—the body's resources diverted to the conversion, to the growing of the chitinous interface, to the neural restructuring that made the telepathic overlap possible. She looked like someone slowly being hollowed out and refilled with something that wasn't quite the original material.

Xi Pei was there too, in his corner, always in his corner. His suit was simpler—fewer layers accumulated, the least colorful of any of them. He'd integrated more slowly than the rest. Grandma had commented on this with what Cass now understood as scientific disappointment. *This one's nervous system is stubborn,* she'd said directly into his skull. *But the stubbornness is located in a fascinating place. Not refusal. Something else. Something administrative.* Even telepathically, she'd sounded almost amused.

And Raj.

Raj was wearing something that barely qualified as a human garment. His form was wrapped in layer upon layer of bio-suit material—teal that faded into iridescent greens, then into that pure bioluminescent white that Cass had only seen on the squids themselves. When he moved, color traveled across the surface of his skin like a school of phosphorescent fish. His temples were no longer just the chitinous nubs of a recently implanted human. They were beginning to branch. Separate structures were forming—not quite tentacles, not quite antennae. Something in between.

"Oh, good," Raj said when he saw Cass. His voice had changed. It was still human-shaped, still produced by vocal cords and lungs, but it carried underneath it a secondary frequency—a harmonic that shouldn't have been possible, a color-sound that made Cass's new neural pathways fire in patterns that had no name.

"What's happening?" Cass asked.

"The collection begins," Grandma's voice bloomed directly in his skull. Not just words anymore—the entire contextual field around the words, the emotional and mathematical weight of them, the cascade of associated imagery. He understood that they were moving into position around Earth. He understood that the net was ready. He understood, with a certainty that felt like memory but had to be foreign knowledge arriving through the implant, that the harvesting was going to happen in the next twelve hours.

He understood also, without being told, that he was going to watch it happen.

"How many?" Xi Pei asked quietly.

"Forty-seven in the initial sweep," Grandma said. "The collection apparatus is a delta configuration. Two nets. The residential zone will yield the highest volume."

Numbers arrived with the statement—not spoken, but felt. Cass saw them as a geometric lattice, as probability distributions, as a kind of mathematical elegance that made his stomach turn.

Raj was smiling.

"The residential zone," Irina said. The words came out flat, diagnostic. "You're taking them while they're sleeping."

"The sleep-state yields the highest survival rate for implantation," Grandma said. "The nervous system is already in a reduced consciousness state. The shock is minimized." She paused. "Would you prefer we use a different methodology?"

Irina didn't answer.

"I have reviewed the human data," Grandma continued. This was directed at all of them now, Cass could feel it spreading through the space like something visible. "The humans we have collected have demonstrated varying levels of attunement. Dylan—Raj, I mean—" A pause. A correction that wasn't quite a correction, a sense of something being acknowledged. "Raj has integrated at a rate I would not have predicted. This suggests that the initial sample group was not representative. The new cohort will provide additional data points."

"On what?" Cass heard himself ask.

The color that came back was so bright it burnt.

"On what your species is capable of becoming," Grandma said. "Come."

---

The observation deck was a section of hull that Grandma had grown transparent—not glass, but something biological that let light through while maintaining structural integrity. From here they could see the planet spinning below, blue and white and swirled with browns and grays that Cass recognized as continents, as weather systems, as the physical place he'd been stolen from.

The net was already deployed.

It was a structure that made no sense to the human eye. It looked like nothing—like a pattern of absence in the water, like a mathematical formula expressed in negative space. Cass could feel it though, through the implant, through the newer, deeper channels of awareness that had opened in his skull. It was a boundary. A trap. A beautifully engineered snare that would trigger on heat signatures and neural activity and the particular electromagnetic signature that human nervous systems produced.

"The net closes in forty-eight minutes," Grandma said. "The prey-capture phase will last approximately eight seconds. The transport phase will last six hours. The initial implantation phase will begin immediately upon arrival in the stasis chamber."

Prey-capture. The word landed like a stone.

"How many will die in the transport?" Xi Pei asked.

"Three to seven," Grandma said without hesitation. "The acceleration creates shearing stress in biological systems not adapted for rapid velocity changes. It is unavoidable. We have optimized the transport configuration to minimize this loss, but the mathematics are firm. Three to seven."

Three to seven humans dead before they ever woke up. Before they ever had a chance to understand what was happening. Just hours ago they were in their beds, in their homes, in that world, and then—

The needle behind Cass's eye screamed.

He gasped, dropped to his knees. The pain was bright and specific, a needle through the cerebellum, a wire vibrating at a frequency that no human body should have been able to survive. Raj caught him as he fell, held him up—and through the physical contact, Cass felt something else arriving. Not pain. Information. A context for the pain.

Grandma was confused. She was analyzing his resistance. She was noting it. She was routing the observation through channels that Cass understood, now, were connected to the central crew hub, to Delta Del Squared, to the other squids aboard and in communication across light-years that didn't matter because they were connected through the mathematical telepathy that moved faster than distance.

They were all watching. They were all noting that Cass was experiencing something other humans weren't supposed to experience.

Something that looked almost like objection.

"Interesting," Grandma said. "The neural resistance is stronger than predicted. I am routing this data to my research files. This may indicate—"

"Stop," Cass said.

The word came out in his own voice, purely human, with no harmonic underneath. Just a sound. Just a request.

Grandma's presence in his skull didn't pause. Didn't acknowledge. She continued her observation, continued her routing of the data, continued her work.

Because she didn't understand the word stop. Because to her, the objection of prey was just another data point. Because in her framework, his resistance was the same as the resistance of a mouse caught in a trap—neurologically interesting, scientifically notable, and ultimately irrelevant to the function of the trap.

The net was closing. Forty-five minutes now.

Raj was still holding him. Through the contact, Cass could feel the bioluminescence moving under his skin—could feel the joy there, the genuine delight that Raj was experiencing as the collection progressed. There was no cruelty in it. Just the happiness of someone watching a process work. The happiness of someone who had already decided that there was no longer any meaningful difference between being human and being something else.

"You're fighting it," Raj said quietly. His voice was that strange double-frequency thing now, like he was speaking in two languages at once. "That's cute. Stop fighting it. It's better this way."

Cass tried to pull away but couldn't. His muscles weren't responding properly. The implant was affecting the signal pathway between his brain and his limbs, reinterpreting his commands, routing them through new neural circuits that didn't care what he was trying to do.

The observation deck smelled like copper and salt and something chemical underneath, like the taste of the implant antennae when he accidentally bit down on them. Outside, the net was tightening. He could see it now—not as absence but as presence, as a field of purpose, as a structure of intention that was reaching down toward the planet and closing slowly.

"How long?" he asked.

"Until they hit the net?" Raj said. "Thirty-nine minutes. Until we start implanting them? Maybe six hours after that."

"And you're okay with this."

Raj's smile was luminous.

"Okay isn't the right word," Raj said. "It's not about okay. It's about whether you understand what you're becoming. I understand. Grandma says I'm the fastest integration she's ever seen. She's proud of me. And I'm proud of me. And honestly, man? For the first time in my life, I'm proud of being alive. That's worth something."

---

Irina was in the laboratory when they brought Cass back down.

The lab was Grandma's domain—a space of suspended animation pods containing what Cass had been told were "specimens from previous collections." The pods were arranged in rows like sarcophagi. Inside them, things that had once been human. Things that hadn't survived the conversion. Things that had survived in shapes that made Irina's expression go flat and analytical every time she looked at them.

"What's happening?" Irina asked. She was working on something—taking measurements of a bio-membrane sample with instruments that had been grown for the purpose. Tools without names. Tools that didn't exist in any human language.

"The collection begins in thirty-eight minutes," Cass said.

Irina's hands went still.

"How many?" she asked.

"Forty-seven."

"Initial casualty estimate?"

"Three to seven in transport. Higher numbers during implantation." Cass heard himself reporting this with Grandma's precision. Heard himself thinking of it in Grandma's framework. The numbers were just numbers. The casualties were mathematical necessities, not tragedies. This was how you talked about it if you wanted to be rational about it.

"How many are children?" Irina asked.

The question caught something in his implant. Some resonance between his human instinct and the data that was arriving through the telepathic channels. He could feel Grandma's awareness turning toward the question, could feel her accessing the scan data she'd already pulled from the planet's residential zones.

"Approximately eighteen of the forty-seven are below human sexual maturity age," Grandma's voice said directly into both their skulls. "The younger ones often have less developed nervous systems. They adapt faster. The survival rate for the zero-to-twelve age group is approximately ninety-two percent. For the thirteen-to-eighteen age group, approximately seventy-eight percent. For adults, approximately sixty-one percent."

Irina set down her tools.

"We have to stop this," she said.

The statement hung in the air like something physical.

"Stop what?" Grandma asked.

"The collection. The implantation. The entire operation." Irina turned to face them directly. "I'm refusing. I'm invoking whatever protocol you have for crew members who cannot continue with the work."

"There is no such protocol," Grandma said. "You are valuable. You will continue."

"I'm choosing not to."

Silence. A genuine silence this time, not the absence of sound but the presence of something thinking. Of something processing information that didn't fit into its framework.

"You cannot choose," Grandma said finally. "Your nervous system has been modified. The choice-making circuits have been overlaid with additional pathways that guarantee compliance with certain fundamental directives. You are free to choose only within the parameters of your own continued integration. Beyond that parameter—"

She didn't finish the sentence. Didn't need to. Cass could feel the implication arriving through the implant like a color flooding into a closed space. If Irina tried to refuse beyond the boundaries she'd been given, the implant would simply override the refusal. The signal wouldn't get from her brain to her body. The intention would dissolve somewhere in the new neural pathways.

"How long have you had that control?" Irina asked.

"Since the third implantation layer," Grandma said. "It was necessary. Your species requires containment at the neurological level. Otherwise, you attempt self-harm. You attempt escape. You develop ideological resistance. All of these things create inefficiency."

Irina was breathing in a specific pattern now. In through the nose, out through the mouth, like she was trying to control something internal that wanted to spiral. Cass recognized it because he'd done the same thing in the first days after waking up with the implant.

"How long does it take?" Irina asked.

"Before you accept it?" Grandma's presence was almost gentle now. Solicitous. "It varies. Raj has accepted it fully. You are approaching acceptance. The mathematician will arrive at acceptance more slowly—the bureaucratic pathways in his mind make him cautious. But all of you, eventually, reach the point where the resistance becomes too exhausting to maintain. And then you move forward."

She said it like it was a kindness. Like it was salvation.

The observation deck chimed.

"Thirty minutes to net closure," Grandma said. "I require your presence at the transport bay. The initial specimens will need examination upon arrival. Irina, you will coordinate the accelerant infusions. Cass, you will assist with the initial implantation preparations. Raj will manage the specimen flow."

Cass felt his legs moving.

He wasn't consciously deciding to move. The intention to move was just arriving from somewhere deeper in his nervous system, arriving fully formed, arriving with the weight of mathematics behind it. His body obeyed without waiting for his conscious mind to catch up.

Irina was still standing at the laboratory table, hands flat against the surface, breathing in that controlled pattern.

"Come," Grandma said.

And Irina came.

---

The transport bay was vast. It was the largest space in the ship, a cathedral carved from living tissue, with walls that pulsed in rhythm with the ship's acceleration. There were suspension tanks here—dozens of them, empty now, but already being filled with a nutrient solution that glowed faintly in the darkness. There were tools arranged on walls that had grown specifically to hold them. There were surfaces stained with what had to be blood, though Cass tried not to think about from what, or from whom.

Raj was orchestrating something with his hands.

He was no longer just human. The bioluminescence across his skin was active now, streaming with color, and through it Cass could feel him communicating with Grandma in that double-layer language. Not telepathy alone, not bioluminescence alone, but both at once, information flowing through two channels simultaneously. His voice was doing something almost the same thing—human words carrying underneath them a kind of harmonic subtext that made the entire bay resonate.

"The nets are reading stable," Raj said, addressing both humans and Grandma. "The prey density in sector three is higher than predicted. We should expect approximately fifty-two instead of forty-seven. The apparatus is compensating for the additional mass load. Transport velocity is within optimal parameters."

He was speaking like someone with technical training that he'd never had. Like someone who'd learned the entire operational context of the collection apparatus by osmosis through the implant connection.

"Twenty minutes," Grandma said.

The wait was the worst part.

Cass stood in the transport bay with Irina and Xi Pei and Raj, and he waited. The bay filled slowly with a kind of anticipation that was almost alive. He could feel it moving through the water around them, feel it in the rhythm of the ship's acceleration, feel it in the way the suspension tanks began to glow brighter in preparation.

Xi Pei was still quiet. His suit was still dull. He hadn't accumulated the layers that the others had, hadn't integrated as far into the system. But there was something happening behind his eyes now—something calculating, something that had nothing to do with the implant and everything to do with the man himself. He was watching. He was observing the bay, the process, the machinery. He was measuring it against something inside his own head.

"Ten minutes," Grandma said.

The needle behind Cass's eye pulsed again. Not pain this time. Something closer to anticipation. Something that was learning to enjoy this.

He recognized it for what it was: the part of him that was no longer entirely human, preparing to feed.

"I don't want to do this," he said quietly.

Raj looked at him with something that might have been pity.

"Yeah," Raj said. "But you're going to anyway."

The observation deck chimed again.

"Five minutes," Grandma said. "Brace for the arrival."

And in the suspension tanks, the nutrient solution began to flow.
