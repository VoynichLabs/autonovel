# Chapter 15: The Copper Argument

The needle pulsed behind Cass's left eye. Once. Twice. Steady as a metronome now, the rhythm settling into something almost comfortable. He'd stopped flinching at it three days ago. Now it was just there, the way a bad tooth becomes the baseline you measure everything else against.

His mother stood in the doorway of the basement, her hand on the frame. She hadn't come down in almost twenty-four hours. Cass could tell because the angle of the light from the narrow window had shifted twice since she'd left—morning to noon to evening, the sun moving its rectangle across the concrete floor. She was looking at him the way you look at something you used to recognize and suddenly don't.

"You're still you," she said.

It wasn't a question. He understood this. She was testing the shape of the words, seeing if they held.

Cass was sitting on the workbench beside the bronze. His father's bronze. The metal had gone soft in the last few days—not melted, not exactly, but responsive. When he laid his hand on it, it held the impression for a moment before settling back to its original form. Like touching something that breathed. The rhythm inside him—the needle pulse—seemed to sync with the metal's response, as if the thing in his skull was sending the metal messages through the copper wiring his father had threaded into the bronze's core.

"I'm still here," Cass said. He meant it. He also understood why it didn't feel like the truth, which was its own kind of truth.

His mother came down the stairs slowly, one hand on the rail. The basement smelled like it always had—mineral and old concrete and something else now, something biological that came off the wiring. The implant at his temple had grown again. He could feel the chitinous texture under his fingertips when he reached up. It didn't hurt anymore. That was the strange part. Pain had been replaced by a constant low vibration, like standing near a speaker playing something just below audible range.

She stopped three feet away from him. Not afraid, exactly. Careful. The way you approach an animal that might still recognize you but shouldn't be trusted.

"Does it hurt?" she asked.

"No."

The needle pulsed. Once. Twice.

"What does it feel like?"

Cass tried to answer this. He opened his mouth. The words didn't come. What did it feel like? Like standing in a room where a conversation was happening in a language he was learning to read. Like the bronze underneath his hand and the copper in the walls and the electrical system of the house were all one thing now, and he was the frequency that connected them. Like being asleep and awake at the same time, neither state quite overwriting the other.

"Like I'm listening to something I've always been hearing," he said finally. "But now I can understand it."

His mother's face did something complicated. Relief crossed it. Horror followed. They occupied the same moment.

"The signal's still broadcasting," she said. It wasn't a question either.

"Yes."

"To where?"

Cass gestured toward the ceiling. Up through the walls. Out past the house. The copper piping carried it, and the copper wiring in his father's bronze, and the electrical grid underneath the town. The signal was riding all of it like a voice riding a wire. He could feel it moving. The implant at his temple pulsed in synchronization with each transmission.

His mother pulled a wooden stool over and sat. She looked older. Time had done something to her face in the last seventy-two hours that seemed wrong to compress into that span. She'd aged sideways, somehow. Not in visible years but in the distribution of her expression—less of it on the left side, more focused and concentrated on the right, as if half of her was being recalibrated.

"Your father called the emergency line," she said. "They came. They found nothing wrong with the house. No fire. No gas. No electrical problem. They told him he was wasting their time."

Cass could picture it. The emergency responders moving through the house with their instruments. The instruments finding nothing because there was nothing to find if you didn't know what you were looking for. The signal wasn't chemical. It wasn't thermal. It was something being carried through the wiring itself, through the bronze, through the house as a medium. A message riding infrastructure that wasn't designed to carry messages.

"Did he believe them?" Cass asked.

"He wanted to. He really wanted to." His mother looked at her hands. "I could see him wanting to. But he's been in this basement. He's been touching the bronze. He can feel it working. So he didn't really believe. He just needed someone official to tell him it was fine."

The needle pulsed harder. Cass grabbed the edge of the workbench. The pain was sudden and bright—not behind his eye anymore but through it, a spike of white light that made him see double. He waited for it to pass. It did. It always did.

"Cass?"

He held up one hand. His mother reached out like she might touch him, then didn't.

When the pain receded, it left a taste in his mouth. Copper. He was tasting copper.

"They're listening," he said. "The crew. They hear the signal. They're listening and they're trying to understand what kind of broadcast needs this much infrastructure. What kind of intelligence is building a transmitter out of a house."

"What kind is it?" His mother's voice had gone very quiet.

Cass looked at his hands. The chitinous nub at his temple had grown enough that he could see it in peripheral vision now—a hard ridge of something translucent, shot through with colors he'd never seen in nature. Colors that hurt to look at directly. Colors that made more sense when he wasn't looking at them, when he could just feel the radiation they were giving off.

"I don't know yet," he said. "But they're curious. They're very curious."

His mother stood abruptly. She walked to the far side of the basement where his father's work table was. The tools were neatly arranged—soldering iron, tongs, the jeweler's glass, the fine brushes for applying patina. His father had a way with precision. Everything had a place. Everything meant something. The order was the work, in a sense. The arrangement itself was part of the craft.

She picked up the soldering iron. It was unplugged. She turned it over in her hands like it might tell her something.

"Your father's making something new," she said. "Up in the workshop. Something that was supposed to be a wind chime but isn't anymore. He won't let me see it. He says it's not ready. But I've heard him working. The sound is different."

Cass understood. His father had a sound signature for different projects. Wind chimes were high and clear and repetitive. This was something else. He could almost hear it from here—a lower frequency, something that had presence.

"A receiver," Cass said.

His mother set the soldering iron down gently. "What?"

"He's building a receiver. Not to listen to the signal. To talk to it."

The needle pulsed. Pulsed again. The rhythm was accelerating. Whatever was happening in the ship above the world, whatever was happening with the crew and their instruments and their analysis of the broadcast, it was reaching some kind of crescendo. Cass could feel it in the implant. Feel it in the way the bronze underneath his hand had started vibrating at a frequency too low to hear but entirely possible to feel.

His mother looked at the stairs. "I should tell him to stop."

"You should. Yes."

She didn't move.

"But he won't," Cass said. "And you know he won't. Because he felt it too, didn't he? He felt something wrong the moment you called him home from work. He felt the house changing. So he came down here and he touched the bronze and he knew."

"Knew what?"

"That something else was using his craft. That his skill, his precision, his understanding of how to make metal sing—that it was all being used for something he didn't consent to. Something was speaking his language. And he couldn't not respond."

His mother sat back down on the stool. She looked very small there, in the shadow of the fluorescent light, with the basement full of tools his father had chosen over decades. Every one of them a piece of his attention. Every one a small monument to the idea that you could make something true just by making it well enough.

"Is he going to hurt anyone?" she asked.

Cass considered this. The needle pulsed. He felt the broadcast traveling through the wiring, up the walls, out into the world. He felt the crew receiving it, analyzing it, beginning to understand that they weren't looking at an anomaly. They were looking at a civilization learning its own language for the first time. A civilization made of bronze and copper and the hands of craftspeople, teaching itself to speak.

"No," he said. "I don't think anyone's going to get hurt."

His mother's laugh was small and bitter. "That's not an answer, Cass. That's you not wanting to say no."

She was right. He could feel the distinction clearly. She wasn't asking whether the broadcast would cause harm in some abstract sense. She was asking whether his father's new receiver, and whatever it would do, and whatever would come after it was complete—whether all of that could be contained.

The honest answer was: he didn't know.

The needle pulsed. Once. Twice. And then it didn't pulse again for a long time.

The silence in the basement felt like something breaking. Like the moment when a frequency drops too low to hear but the whole room still knows it's there.

"They want to meet him," Cass said. "Your son. The second one. The one who could only ever do one thing well because everything else required language he didn't have. They want to understand how his particular kind of precision works. How his exactness transformed when something else started using his craft."

His mother's face went very still.

"The crew," Cass continued, "they don't have mechanics. Everything they do is biological. They grow things. They don't assemble. They don't join. They don't make metals sing. So they don't understand how your son could be infected with an implant and still use tools. Still feel the precision. Still recognize when something is asking him to make it beautiful. Most creatures—when you invade their nervous system like this—they break. But your son is using it. He's using it to extend what he already was."

Cass could feel the broadcast opening. The frequency widening. The ship above was preparing to receive something. A person, maybe. A body. Or maybe just the schematic. Maybe they didn't need the physical thing. Maybe they just needed the information.

His mother stood very quickly. "No. No, you tell them no. You tell them—whatever they're planning—"

"I don't think I can do that," Cass said.

She grabbed his shoulder. Her hand was warm. It shook slightly. "Then I will. I'll call someone. I'll tell them there are visitors coming. I'll make it impossible for them to—"

The needle pulsed once, sharp and violent.

Cass gasped. The pain was cleaner this time. Not diffuse. Surgical. As if someone was making a very precise cut through a very specific part of his brain.

His mother let go. "Cass?"

He opened his eyes. He'd closed them without noticing. The basement was still the basement. The bronze was still the bronze. But the signal was different now. It had changed. It was no longer broadcasting outward. It was receiving. Waiting. Open.

"It's okay," he said. "Tell Dad it's okay. Tell him they're not going to hurt me. They're going to ask me questions, and I'm going to help them understand, and in exchange—"

He stopped. He didn't know what the exchange was. But he could feel it there in the broadcast, like a piece of paper folded inside an envelope. A negotiation. A trade.

"In exchange for what?" his mother whispered.

"In exchange for not culling the entire species. For understanding that we're not vermin. That we can make things they can't. That we have a value in the hierarchy that's outside of food and pets."

The needle pulsed. Once. Twice. A new rhythm, slower than before. More certain.

His mother sat back down on the stool. She looked at her hands again, and this time she kept looking. She didn't look at Cass anymore, not directly. And he understood that this was a choice. That she was choosing not to watch him becoming something else, because if she did, she'd have to acknowledge that he was already becoming it.

The bronze underneath his hand vibrated at a frequency just barely below sound. His father's hands had learned to feel those frequencies. Had learned to listen to them. Had learned to make them into shapes the world could hold.

And now Cass could do that too. Not his father's way. Not quite. But in the same language. Close enough that the creatures in the ship above would understand what they were looking at when they finally came.

His mother's breathing was very quiet in the basement.

The signal expanded.
