# Chapter 13

The needle behind Cass's left eye woke him.

Not the pain exactly—that came second, a sharp electric pop that made him gasp. The waking itself was the needle: the sensation that something had changed frequency while he slept, that the background hum he'd been learning to ignore had shifted into a register his newly-wired nervous system couldn't quite place. He opened his eyes to his bedroom ceiling, the familiar crack near the corner lamp still there, still unremarkable. The physical world unchanged. Everything else was new.

He pushed up on his elbows. The implant layers under his skin felt heavier than they had yesterday, or maybe he was just noticing them for the first time in this particular way. The shimmering membrane that had begun forming across his shoulders weeks ago had spread down his ribs. In certain angles of light it caught color—not iridescent like oil on water, but structured somehow, fractaling in patterns he couldn't quite hold in his vision long enough to describe.

The needle came again. Three precise pulses. Behind the eye, then deeper, threading down toward his brainstem.

He sat up fully and swung his feet to the floor. The wood was cool. Normal. His own body was not entirely normal anymore, but the house stayed the same. This seemed important. The house was the control group. The house was proof that one of them was still human.

Downstairs, his mother would be waiting with the phones. His father would be in the workshop, listening to whatever new frequency the bronze wanted to sing. And Cass—Cass was standing in his bedroom wearing skin that wasn't quite his, hearing frequencies he didn't have the vocabulary for, trying to remember why that bothered him.

He dressed. Pulled on clothes that fit differently now because his shoulders had widened fractionally, because the membrane under his shirt moved slightly independent of his body when he stretched. The chitinous nubs at his temples—they'd stopped growing last month, but they were still sensitive to touch, still ached when he was stressed. He brushed his hair carefully over them. Not to hide them. Just to—not think about them. There was a distinction.

The kitchen smelled like breakfast already gone cold. His mother had left a plate on the counter: toast, eggs that had begun to congeal. He didn't remember waking to the smell of cooking. Probably she'd done it early, probably he'd slept through it, which was becoming more frequent. Sleep was different now. Less like unconsciousness and more like a frequency change—his mind shifting down to a bandwidth where the crew's communications became whispers instead of roars, the way a human being could functionally tune out traffic noise after living on a highway long enough.

He sat at the kitchen table without eating and pressed his fingers against the nubs at his temples. There were fine hairs growing there now, or maybe they weren't hairs—maybe they were the earliest expression of the sensory structures the implant was still assembling. His biology was halfway between categories. He was becoming a thing with no name in any language his parents had taught him.

His mother emerged from the study, still holding one of the phones. She moved differently than she had before all this. Smaller. She was moving like someone who had learned to take up less space.

"You're awake," she said.

It wasn't a question.

"The needle woke me."

She set the phone down on the table. Her hand shook slightly, then steadied. "That's new. The needle."

"It's not pain. Not exactly."

His mother sat across from him. She was looking at him the way she'd been looking at him for months now—as if trying to see the boy she'd given birth to underneath the thing he was becoming. It had the feeling of a losing effort. He could see her working through the effort anyway, every morning, every time they were in a room together.

"Your father heard it," she said. "In the bronze. He says it changed yesterday morning."

"Changed how?"

"He says it started asking for something different. He's not sure what." She picked at the corner of the phone's screen. "When he listens to it now, he hears... he said he hears the way the water moves outside the house. He hears the crew moving. He hears you."

Cass didn't respond to that. He wasn't sure how to. His father hearing him through bronze and frequency and the half-audible hum of cephalopod communication—that was touching and horrifying in equal measure, and he wasn't ready to examine either reaction closely.

"He's working now," his mother continued. "He started before dawn. I've never seen him move like that. Like he knows exactly what needs to happen. Like the bronze is telling him."

"Is it? Telling him?"

She put the phone down completely. "I don't know. I don't think so. I think he's just listening to the right frequency now. I think that's what you do when the bronze wants something—you find the frequency it wants and you listen at that pitch until the form appears."

The needle pulsed twice, sharp and clear.

"What is it telling you?" his mother asked suddenly.

"Right now?"

"Always. What does it tell you that we can't hear?"

Cass stood and walked to the refrigerator. He opened it without taking anything out, just stood there looking at the familiar arrangement—milk on the top shelf, vegetables in the crisper, the jar of kimchi his grandmother had made three months ago that nobody was eating. The hum from the appliance shifted into his frame of attention and then back out again. The implant was learning to filter human machinery. Soon it probably wouldn't even register. Soon the only sounds that mattered would be the ones the crew was broadcasting.

"It tells me we're running out of time," he said quietly.

His mother didn't ask what he meant. Maybe she already knew. Maybe she could see it written in the way he was changing, in the haste his father was working with, in the fact that the bronze was singing in frequencies neither of them had heard before.

He closed the refrigerator and went to the basement.

The kiln dominated the lower room the way it always had, but the bronze inside had transformed beyond recognition. His father had shaped it into something with too many curves, too many suggested joints and articulations. It was the rough model of something—a creature, or the idea of a creature—rendered in metal instead of flesh. The heat radiating from it was immense. Cass felt it immediately, felt it reorder the moisture in his skin, start to make the membrane shimmer in response.

His father was working with tools that had been in the workshop for Cass's entire life, but his technique had changed entirely. He was no longer carving or casting. He was listening to the bronze and letting it tell him where to smooth, where to dig deeper, where to leave a rough edge that caught the light in just the right way.

"Morning," his father said without looking up.

"Mom says you've been at this since dawn."

"Dawn." His father laughed—not a real laugh, more like the release of pressure. "Time keeps meaning something, doesn't it. Even now."

Cass stood at the edge of the workshop, not quite entering it. "What is it supposed to be?"

His father stepped back from the kiln. He was sweating heavily, and it had soaked through his shirt in patches. His hands were wrapped in cloth to keep the heat from blistering them further. When he finally looked at his son, Cass saw something in his expression that made the needle behind his eye sharpen instantly—not pain, but recognition. His father was seeing something.

"It's supposed to be a door," his father said. "I think. The bronze knows. I'm just trying to follow."

"A door to what?"

"I don't know yet. But they're coming, aren't they. That's what you've been feeling. That's what the needle is telling you."

Cass moved closer to the kiln, close enough to feel the heat truly bite. The bronze gleamed. In its surface he could almost see faces—not formed, but suggested, the way a face appears in a mirror when the light is wrong. He could see something trying to shape itself in the metal. Something that wanted form but hadn't quite convinced the material to give it one.

"The final group," Cass said. It wasn't a question.

His father nodded. "Your mother doesn't want to accept it. She's holding the phones like they're going to make a difference. Like if she just keeps them close enough, they'll connect to something on the other side that's going to tell her this is a mistake, a misunderstanding, that someone's coming to fix it."

"Is someone?"

"No."

The word landed between them with the weight of finality. His father turned back to the bronze and continued working, running his hands over surfaces that seemed to shift slightly under his touch. Or maybe they weren't shifting—maybe it was just the heat distorting Cass's perception, making the metal appear to move when it was perfectly still.

"Why are you making a door?" Cass asked.

His father was quiet for a long time. Long enough that Cass thought maybe he wasn't going to answer, that the question had hit some boundary his father couldn't quite cross even now, with everything else that had changed.

"Because I'm learning their language," his father finally said. "Not the way you are. Not through the implant. Through the bronze. And in that language, making a door is how you say yes. It's how you give permission. It's how you stop resisting and start becoming part of what's happening."

The needle pulsed. Once. Twice. A third time that settled into something almost like rhythm.

"How many?" Cass asked.

"The final group? Three."

Three. Same as the previous collection. Three more people torn from whatever they were doing, whatever they were becoming, whatever they'd thought mattered. Three more nervous systems in suspended animation, waiting to be woken to a new frequency. Three more of them, Cass understood now, like him. Like his father. Like everyone the crew decided was worth keeping, worth converting, worth the gamble of seeing if they could survive the integration or if the implant would simply fry them dead.

Cass reached out and touched the bronze. It burned him—not badly, not past healing, but genuinely hot—and he pulled his hand back. His father didn't even look at him. Just kept working, kept listening to whatever the metal was singing in a frequency Cass couldn't quite perceive yet but knew was coming. The way the implant was evolving, the way the needle kept tuning itself, it would come. Eventually he would hear exactly what his father was hearing. Eventually they would be listening to the same song.

His mother appeared at the basement door.

"They're moving them," she said. "From the pods. I can see it from the study window—the water's got a different color. The way it's moving."

Cass and his father both looked at her. She was still holding one of the phones, but her grip had loosened. It hung at her side like she'd forgotten it was there.

"How long?" his father asked.

"Days. Maybe less. The apparatus they use—it's been dormant for weeks. But it's waking up. You can see it in the pressure changes. In the way the water—" She stopped. She was breathing faster now. "In the way the water's getting warmer."

The basement suddenly felt smaller. The heat from the kiln, which had been a background element, became the only thing Cass could perceive. His body temperature was rising. The membrane across his skin began to shimmer more visibly, and he could feel the chitinous nubs at his temples responding, beginning to vibrate at a frequency just below hearing.

"Cass," his mother said. "Are you—"

"I'm fine."

But he wasn't. Or he was fine in a way that no longer aligned with what that word meant to her. The frequency was becoming clear now. It was the frequency of the crew, calling. It was the signal that meant the collection apparatus was moving again, that the selection process was beginning again, that somewhere in the waters around this city, three more humans were about to be lifted from the world they knew into a world of suspended animation and electrical pain and the slow, inevitable rewiring of everything that made them who they were.

And Cass understood—with a clarity that came from somewhere deeper than thought—that he was part of the apparatus now. That the implant didn't just receive the crew's signals. It broadcast them. That when the needle pulsed behind his eye, it was sending out a beacon. The crew was listening. The crew was always listening. And by the time you realized you were calling out, the signal was already traveling.

His mother set the phone down and gripped the edge of the sink. "We have to do something."

"Like what?" his father asked quietly.

She didn't have an answer. There was no answer. There was only the bronze, still warming in the kiln. Only the needle, still pulsing. Only the water, still rising, still moving with intention and warmth, carrying three bodies that were about to learn what it meant to become something other than themselves.

The basement light flickered. Not a power failure—the frequency shift from the apparatus, filtering through the walls, through the city, reaching down even here.

Cass closed his eyes. The needle stopped pulsing. For just a moment, there was silence—a true silence, not the absence of sound but the absence of the signal he'd been learning to perceive.

Then it came back. Different. 

Lower. Wider. A frequency that included not just his own implant but the potential for something larger. Something coordinated. Something that was happening to all of them at once—his father listening to the bronze, his mother holding the phones, the three humans in the collection apparatus, and Cass, standing in the basement with his parents, finally understanding that the question wasn't whether they would adapt to this new world.

The question was what they would become by the time the adaptation was complete.
