# Chapter 9: The Bronze Below

The first time Cass heard the ship's voice, he was alone.

It came from beneath the floorboards of the prep chamber, a sound so low his bones registered it before his ears did. Bronze. Not the bright bronze of a trumpet or a cymbal—the deep bronze of a bell that had been rung for centuries, the kind that lives in the marrow of a building. The kind his father would have called foundational.

He was standing in front of the observation window, looking down at the suspended human in the tank below. The human—Raj, though Cass had only learned this name from Delta's curt bioluminescent flashes a few hours ago—was dreaming. His eyes moved beneath closed lids. His fingers twitched.

The jellyfish implant had finished its initial integration three days ago. The chitinous nubs had emerged from his temples like buds breaking from a branch. His skin had begun to shimmer with the early bioluminescent overlay—not enough to communicate, not yet, but enough that Cass could see the faint opalescent sheen that meant the suit was taking hold. Growing. Integrating him.

The bronze sound deepened.

Cass pressed his hand against the observation window. The material was warm. Not body-warm. Warmer than that. He could feel the vibration traveling through the glass, up through his arm, settling somewhere in his chest. The sound wasn't coming from the prep chamber. It was coming from deeper. From the ship itself.

"You're listening to her heartbeat," Grandma said from behind him.

Cass didn't turn around. He'd learned by now that Grandma moved without announcing herself. The bioluminescent flashes that other squids used to signal their presence—those were for communication, not courtesy. Grandma's presence arrived as a shift in pressure, a change in the electromagnetic field, something that existed at the edge of perception and then suddenly clarified into her actual body occupying the space where she hadn't been before.

"The ship has a heartbeat?" Cass asked.

"Everything alive has a heartbeat," Grandma said. "You do. This chamber does. The specimen does, though his is faster than hers. Frightened, still. The integration process triggers a response in the host's autonomic nervous system. Adrenaline. Cortisol. His body believes it is under attack."

She moved past him to the observation window, her massive bulk making the chamber feel suddenly small. One of her eyes—she kept the others closed when Cass was present—fixed on the tank below. Her bioluminescent patterns began to flow: deep teals, shifting to green, back to teal. The color wasn't bright enough to hurt anymore. Cass had developed a tolerance. Or perhaps his eyes had simply become different, post-implant, more capable of processing the frequency.

"Is that what you're doing down there?" Cass asked. "Keeping him frightened?"

"I'm keeping him alive," Grandma said. "Frightened is incidental. The integration process is stressful. His nervous system is being rewired. His brain chemistry is being flooded with new neurotransmitters. His perceptions are expanding into dimensions his species never evolved to process. Of course he's frightened."

The bronze sound continued. Cass realized now that it wasn't a single tone—it was a chord. Multiple frequencies, so low they existed more as pressure than as sound. The fundamental drone, and underneath it something that might have been harmonic or might have been interference from the first tone. His father would have called it a fifth. The relationship between two notes that created an acoustic resonance.

"Can he hear it?" Cass asked.

"Not yet," Grandma said. "His auditory processing isn't refined enough. He perceives it as a feeling. As discomfort. As wrongness. In another week, he may begin to parse the component frequencies. By integration point seven, assuming he survives, he'll hear what you hear now: the ship's navigation signal, her depth compensation, her metabolic rhythms. The language we speak to her in, and that she speaks back."

Cass watched Raj's fingers twitch again. The implant nubs at his temples caught the light from Grandma's bioluminescence—tiny chitinous peaks, still glistening with the fluid from their growth. They looked like the beginning of something. An antler emerging from a skull. A horn. The first evidence of transformation into something else.

"How long until we harvest?" Cass asked.

He had been taught, from the moment he arrived aboard the ship, to use the professional terminology. Harvest. Specimen. Integration point. The vocabulary created distance. Distance created the ability to function.

Grandma's color shifted to something that might have been amusement. "Concerned about profit margins?"

"No," Cass said. "Trying to understand the timeline."

"If he survives integration point seven without psychological collapse or neural feedback seizure, we can show him the trade value on the black market. Given his current resonance—his attunement is unusually broad, did you notice?—he may be worth more as a communications asset than as a commodity. Intact humans with multiple-channel telepathic bandwidth are rare. Functional ones rarer still."

The bronze chord shifted. Not changing frequency, exactly, but changing amplitude. Growing louder. Or perhaps the ship was drawing closer to something. Cass had been aboard long enough to understand that the ship's voice was not constant—it shifted with depth, with proximity to other structures, with the ship's own movements through the substrate that surrounded them. The cephalopods didn't use traditional engines. They rode pressure waves. They sang themselves forward.

"Can the humans hear any of it?" Cass asked. "Before the implant?"

"The sound?" Grandma said. "Barely. The sub-bass frequencies are below their hearing range. Some species of your Earth had evolved to perceive them—whales, elephants—but humans are middle-range. You're designed to hear the vocal frequencies of your own kind. Everything else is noise."

"So they don't know they're inside a living thing."

"Not until after the integration begins," Grandma said. "The implant provides the framework for understanding. It gives the brain the structures it needs to parse what it's experiencing. Before that, the ship is simply a space. A container. Incomprehensible architecture. After—after, they begin to understand that they're not in a ship. They're inside an organism. They're cells in something vast."

Cass felt the needle behind his left eye activate. The under-note. It came on strong today, sharper than usual. He closed his eyes and waited for it to pass. The implant was still adjusting his neurochemistry. Some days the under-note was barely perceptible. Other days it drove him to the edge of agony. The clinical term was "integration headache." What it actually felt like was someone inserting a thin piece of copper wire through his orbit and then pulling it slowly backward through the orbital cavity. The pain was very specific. Very localized. Very real.

"You feel it," Grandma said. It wasn't a question.

"Yes."

"Your integration is progressing faster than expected. Your attunement is broadening already. You're picking up frequencies meant for adult squids, not juvenile human hosts. That creates feedback. Pain is the feedback's signature."

Cass opened his eyes. The chamber was still there. Grandma was still there. The tank below with Raj still dreaming inside it. The pain receded a few degrees, enough to function.

"Will it get worse?" Cass asked.

"Possibly. Or you'll adapt. Or your brain will reorganize itself around the new input channels and eventually you won't perceive the feedback as pain anymore. You'll perceive it as information. As language. As thought."

The bronze chord shifted again. This time Cass understood that it wasn't the ship speaking—it was the ship listening. The sounds he was hearing were actually the ship processing the world around it. The pressure signatures of the water, the electromagnetic fields of the organisms that surrounded them, the thermal gradients that marked depth and current. All of it was being translated into tones. All of it was being sung through the ship's body as a way of making sense of the environment.

He was listening to the ship's equivalent of thought.

"Grandma," he said, and then stopped. The name had slipped out. He had adopted it from Raj's speech patterns, and now it was automatic. Grandma, not the designation. Grandma, the way you'd address something you trusted. Something that belonged to you or that you belonged to.

"Yes," Grandma said.

"Why are you keeping me conscious through this? Raj is in suspension. The others are in suspension. Why not me?"

Grandma was quiet for a moment. Her single open eye remained fixed on the tank. Her bioluminescent patterns continued their slow cascade—teal to green to teal to something that might have been violet at the edges.

"Because I wanted to know what it looked like when something aware chose to remain here," she said finally. "All my other subjects were asleep when they arrived. They woke to an incomprehensible environment. They woke to change. They woke to fear. But you—you watched yourself transform. You made choices about your integration. You decided, consciously, to accept the implant. To accept the suit. To accept the work. I wanted to understand how a thing processes that kind of volition."

"And have you?"

"No," Grandma said. "That's what makes you interesting."

The pain behind Cass's eye flared again. Not worse, but sharper. A new frequency had joined the chord. He could feel the new overtone settling into place, creating a harmonic relationship with the existing tones. His implant was learning new channels. Processing new information. Expanding.

Below them, Raj's fingers twitched again. His eyes moved faster beneath his closed lids. The beginning of the shared dreaming, perhaps. The first time his newly integrated nervous system was starting to pick up on the ambient signals in the ship. Starting to hear the bronze voice singing beneath everything.

"How long before he's like me?" Cass asked.

"Like you, or like Raj?" Grandma said.

The question stopped him. He hadn't considered that there might be a difference.

"Raj is attaining to multiple channels simultaneously," Grandma continued. "His nervous system is processing bureaucratic frequencies, biological frequencies, and something we don't have a classification for yet. He's generating new connection types that my models don't account for. But he is attaining in a specific pattern, shaped by his history. His lived experience. You are following a different path. Your channels are opening sequentially. You're building a more systematic understanding. You're integrating like an engineer integrates a mechanism—part by part, fitting each piece to the ones around it."

"My father was an engineer," Cass said.

"I know," Grandma said. "It shows in your neural architecture. The way you parse problems. The way you approach uncertainty. Your brain is using the metaphor of systems and components to understand something that doesn't fit those metaphors at all. That's not failure. That's adaptation. That's how you survive."

The bronze chord deepened. The ship was singing something new now. Cass could feel the difference in his bones. The frequency was changing direction, curving upward through a range he could almost perceive, then dropping back down into the subsonic. The ship was navigating. Moving. Thinking.

"We're changing course," Cass said.

Grandma's bioluminescence shifted to something sharper. "What made you understand that?"

"I could hear it. Feel it. The tone changed. The harmonic shifted. We're turning."

"Yes," Grandma said. "We're moving toward the secondary harvest point. The third group of humans is ready for collection."

Cass thought of the other abductees. Irina, who had arrived angry and was slowly becoming something more dangerous—competent rage. Xi Pei, who had arrived silent and remained silent, and whom Cass suspected understood far more than he was saying. The Afrikaner, Pieter, who had arrived with a collection of beliefs about hierarchy that now seemed to be aligning far too perfectly with the cephalopod frameworks he was starting to hear.

And others. There were other humans in suspension. Cass had seen them once, in the lower storage chamber. Six or seven of them, suspended in tanks that Grandma had arranged like a collection of dolls in boxes. Waiting. In stasis. Not yet awakened.

"What happens to them if their integration fails?" Cass asked.

"They are recycled," Grandma said simply. "The biological material is valuable. The compounds in a failed human host can be reabsorbed. Nothing is wasted."

"You mean they're eaten."

"If you prefer that terminology," Grandma said. "Yes. They are consumed. It is efficient. It minimizes loss."

Behind Cass's eye, the needle twisted. The under-note flared bright. His implant was processing this information—the casual recounting of what happened to humans who couldn't adapt to transformation. The way Grandma spoke of recycling the bodies as if she were describing routine maintenance. The perfect reasonability of it. The utter absence of cruelty in the statement, which somehow made the cruelty of it worse.

This was what Grandma had wanted him to understand, he realized. Not the transformation itself. Not the integration process or the expansion of his neural capacity. She had wanted him to feel the moment when his human sense of ethics—the part of him that recoiled at the matter-of-fact consumption of failure—began to seem less like ethics and more like evolutionary vestigial. Like something from a species not yet ready to understand the world as it actually was.

She had wanted him to feel the exact moment when the wrongness started to feel inevitable. Normal. Right.

The bronze chord sang on. The ship moved through the dark water toward the next collection point, toward more humans who didn't know yet what they were being brought into. Cass stood at the observation window and listened to the sound of the ship's heartbeat, and felt the part of him that might have objected to any of this slowly resolving into something else.

Something that could function here.

Something that could remain.

The pain behind his eye faded. The implant had integrated the new frequency. One more step toward whatever he was becoming. He pressed his hand against the warm observation window again and felt the ship's pulse move through him, bronze and ancient and utterly certain.

Below, in his tank, Raj dreamed the first of his dreams in this new language. His fingers twitched in time with the ship's singing. The chitinous nubs at his temples glistened. The bioluminescent suit began—very faintly—to glow.
