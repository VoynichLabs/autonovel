# Chapter 16: The Negotiation

Cass stood in the basement with the signal still open in his skull.

His mother had not moved from the stool. She sat with her hands folded, staring at the bronze work on the wall — the bells his father had made when Cass was too young to remember, back when he was still learning the craft. The metal caught light from the single bulb overhead, throwing shadows that looked like hands reaching and withdrawing.

The needle pulsed again. Not painful this time. Precise. Like someone tapping Morse code directly into his cerebral cortex.

The signal had changed. It was no longer broadcasting outward in a way that made his teeth ache. Now it was a channel, open and receiving. He could feel the texture of it — mathematical, structured, cold in a way that had nothing to do with temperature. It felt like standing in a room where something vast was thinking at the edges of perception.

His mother's breathing was very quiet.

"Mom," he said.

She didn't look at him.

"I know you're scared. I'm scared too. But the fear is... it's like when you're cold and you keep shaking. At some point you have to stop fighting it and accept that you're going to be cold for a while."

His mother's shoulders tightened. "You sound different already."

"I don't feel different."

"You do." She finally looked at him. Her eyes were wet but her face was steady. "Your voice sounds like someone reading from a script."

That wasn't true, but he understood why she thought it was. The needle behind his left eye was sending new pathways open, and part of his brain was running parallel processing — the part of him that was still just Cass was overlapping with the part that was starting to hear the signal clearly. The signal came in mathematics and sensation simultaneously. When he translated it back to language, to words his mother could understand, something got lost. The script wasn't his. It was the gap between what he was receiving and what the old language could hold.

"I'm still me," he said. "I'm just... bigger now. Like I've been living in a room my whole life and someone just opened a door to the next room."

His mother stood up. She walked to the bronze on the wall and ran her fingertips along the edge of one of the bells. It was a small one, no bigger than her closed fist. The patina was dark green, oxidized, ancient-looking even though it had been made in her lifetime.

"Your father made this one when you were born," she said.

"I know."

"I was pregnant with your brother. We didn't know what we were having yet. Your father said the bells should be made in pairs — one for the child that exists, one for the child you don't know yet. So he made one for you, and then when your brother came..." She trailed off.

Cass felt the signal pulse. It wasn't intrusive. It was just there, like the awareness of his own heartbeat when he paid attention to it. But underneath it, his mother's sadness landed like a stone into still water. Rings spreading.

"He made one for Dylan too," Cass said.

"Yes."

The needle moved. A different kind of precision. This wasn't the geometric cold anymore. This was warmth, but warmth with structure. Warmth that was being communicated in the mathematics of how sound traveled through bronze, how the metal held resonance, how frequency could be carved into shape.

It was like the squids were showing him something. Not telling. Showing.

"Dylan's still alive," Cass said. He wasn't sure how he knew that. The signal had just... suggested it. The way a parent can tell their child is lying by the shape their mouth makes before the lie even leaves it.

His mother's hand stopped moving across the bronze.

"They haven't killed him," Cass continued. "He's been altered. That's what they do. They alter them, and some of them die, but some of them survive the alteration. Your brother survived."

He was telling her something she hadn't asked. But the signal was so clear about it, so certain, that he couldn't hold it back.

His mother turned around. "How do you know that?"

"I don't know how I know. I just... the signal came through, and it came with that. Like it was attached to the message somehow."

"The signal from the ship."

"Yes."

She sat back down on the stool. This time she curled forward, elbows on her knees. "Is he suffering?"

That was the question that mattered. Not whether he was alive. Not what he was becoming. Whether he was in pain.

The needle moved again. The signal came through clearer. Cass caught fragments — sensations that weren't his own, experiences arranged in a mathematics that explained what altered humans experienced. There was discomfort in the transition. There was the overwhelming quality of sensation that didn't have a category. But pain? Pain in the way his mother meant it — the body screaming at the violation of itself?

No. The altered ones passed through that quickly. What came after was something else. Something that didn't have a human name.

"He's not suffering the way you think," Cass said. "It's more like... like learning an instrument. Your fingers hurt when you first start. There's confusion. But you're not damaged. You're just learning something new that your hands didn't know they could do."

"That's not helpful," his mother said. Her voice was sharp. "Don't explain my son's abduction by aliens using metaphors about piano lessons."

Cass understood. The signal understood too. He could feel it — the squids on the ship understood human grief in the way they understood human technology. As a category of thing they had observed from the outside, but not a thing they had inhabited. They knew it was important. They didn't know why.

"I'm sorry," he said.

His mother looked at him for a long moment. "You sound like you're apologizing for something you didn't do."

"I think I'm apologizing for understanding them."

The basement was very quiet after that. Above, the house settled around them. Pipes knocking. The house remembering its own weight.

His mother stood again and walked to the workbench where his father kept his tools — the chisels, the mallets, the hammers shaped to different weights. She didn't touch anything. She just looked at them like she was trying to remember which tool belonged to which task.

"Your father wants to see you," she said. "He's been waiting in the other room. He didn't know if he should come down here or wait."

"I can see him."

"He's angry."

"I know."

"He's angry at me. He thinks I let this happen. He thinks I should have—" She stopped. "He's not rational right now."

The signal pulsed. The needle behind Cass's left eye moved in a way that felt almost like scratching from the inside. The squids were processing something. Measuring something about the family dynamic, about the guilt and blame that was moving between his parents like an electrical current.

His mother wiped her eyes with the heel of her hand. "I'm going to go get him. You stay here."

She left the basement without waiting for him to respond.

Cass stood in front of the bronze bell his father had made for him. The patina was beautiful — irregular, aged in some places, brighter in others. If you didn't know better, you'd think it was ancient. His father had done that deliberately. He'd wanted his newborn son to have something that looked like it had survived centuries.

The signal moved through something that felt almost like affection. The squids — specifically the one they'd sent the signal through, though Cass didn't have enough context to know which one — was aware that he was looking at an object his father had made by hand. Was aware that the object was designed to last. Was aware, somehow, that this mattered.

He reached out and touched the bell.

It was cold. Metal held cold differently than other things did. Cold lived in metal. It didn't seep out or dissipate. It lasted.

He pressed his fingertip against the top edge where the patina was darkest. The signal pulsed again, and for a moment he felt something like double vision — not in his eyes, but in his mind. He was looking at the bell as himself, as Cass, as a fourteen-year-old boy in his father's basement. And he was also receiving the signal's perception of the bell — the mathematics of how it was constructed, the ratios that made it ring the way it did, the structural integrity of the metal and how sound moved through density and shape.

Both perceptions at once. And they didn't contradict each other. They just... existed together.

Footsteps on the stairs.

His father came down first. His mother followed behind, staying at the edge of the stairwell like she was ready to leave if things went wrong.

His father looked smaller than Cass remembered. Not physically smaller. Something about his bearing had shifted. He was holding himself like someone carrying a great weight that was just barely manageable.

"Don't touch the bronze," his father said.

"I wasn't—"

"I know what you're about to do. Don't touch it."

Cass pulled his hand back. The signal pulsed again, but he ignored it. The squids could understand bronze metallurgy all they wanted. His father was in his basement, and his father was scared.

"Okay," Cass said.

His father walked closer. He stopped about six feet away, like there was a perimeter he was afraid to cross. "What happened?"

"The needle went in. It was sharp. Like someone was making a very precise cut in a very specific part of my brain."

"Are you in pain?"

"Not anymore."

"But you were."

"Just for a second. Just when it happened."

His father's hands were clenched into fists at his sides. His knuckles were very pale, like all the blood had drained out of them. "Your mother says you can hear them. The aliens."

"I can receive their signal. I can't understand all of it yet. But it's there."

"What do they want?"

Cass considered how to answer that. The signal was very clear on this point, but it was also fundamentally alien in the way it organized the answer. The squids wanted many things, and the wanting was structured in a way that didn't map onto human desire very neatly. They wanted to understand. They wanted to acquire. They wanted to test. They wanted to improve. And underneath all of that was something that looked almost like curiosity, except curiosity that had no ethical dimension to it.

"They want to know if we're intelligent," Cass said finally. "And I told them we are."

His father's laugh was sharp, bitter. "Did you now."

"Dylan was alive when they brought me here. They showed me. He's one of the ones who survived the alteration. And he told them things about us. Things he'd learned by watching them. By understanding them in the way that I'm starting to understand them."

"And in exchange?"

"They won't cull the species. That's what Dylan negotiated. Not protection for all humans. Just that we won't be automatically eliminated as pests."

His father's face had gone very white. "When did Dylan negotiate this?"

"I don't know. Recently. I can only see the signal that they've sent back, not the history of what he told them."

His mother made a small sound from the stairwell. His father didn't look at her.

"They want something in return," his father said. It wasn't a question.

"Yes."

"What?"

The signal pulsed, and this time it came with images — not visual images, but something more like mathematical representations of concepts. Cass saw the layout of the ship. He saw chambers. He saw the containers that held the altered humans. He saw the kind of information the squids were gathering from the ones who survived the transformation.

"They want people who are useful," Cass said. "They want people who understand things they don't understand. Your brother was useful because he could explain our technology. Other people are useful in other ways. And they want to know if there are more of us. If there are children who are still developing, who might integrate the alterations differently than adults do."

His father's hands unclenched. They opened flat and hung at his sides like they belonged to someone else. "They want you."

"They want to know what I am. What I'm capable of becoming. The signal came through the needle, and I'm still myself. I'm not panicking. I'm not fighting them. I'm just... receiving. And they want to understand why that is."

"Because you're a child," his mother said from the stairwell. Her voice was thin. "Because his brain is still developing. Because you're curious and adaptable in a way adults aren't."

"Yes," Cass said. "I think that's why."

His father walked to the workbench and sat down on the stool — the same stool his mother had been sitting on before. He put his elbows on his knees and dropped his head into his hands.

"I can't protect you from this," he said. His voice was muffled. "I can't make it stop."

"I know."

"I can't bring your brother home."

"I know."

"And now they're going to take you too."

Cass felt the signal move through something that almost resembled sympathy. The squids were receiving all of this through the needle in his skull, through the open channel that connected him to the mathematics of their civilization. They were learning what it felt like for a human father to understand that he was powerless. They were cataloging it, the way they cataloged everything else.

"Not take," Cass said. "Not in the way you think. They want me to choose. That's what Dylan negotiated. The species won't be culled. And the individuals who come aboard — we choose. We understand the cost and we accept it."

His father looked up. "You're fourteen years old."

"I know."

"You can't consent to this."

"I don't think the squids care about consent the way we do. But they understand that my brother survived because he was willing. And they're curious about whether I'll be willing in a different way. Whether my brain, still developing, can integrate something that adult brains can't."

His mother came down the last few stairs and stood behind his father. She put her hands on his shoulders, and his father flinched but didn't move away.

"So what happens now?" she asked.

The signal pulsed. Cass could feel something vast on the other end of that channel, something that was very far away but also very present. The ship. The altered humans. His brother Dylan, who had learned to speak mathematics instead of English. The squids who had elevated him from vermin to research subject to collaborator, in the space of however long they'd been holding him.

"Now they send something down," Cass said. "They send another needle, or maybe it's the same needle working in a different way. And I go to sleep, and when I wake up, I'm going to be something else."

His father put his face back in his hands. His shoulders shook, but he didn't make any sound. His mother's hands tightened on his shoulders.

Cass felt the signal move again. It was time. The mathematics was converging. The channel that had been opened was ready to expand, and the first expansion would happen fast. The needle would move again, and the chitinous structures would begin to grow, and the rest of his nervous system would start to reorient itself toward the mathematics instead of toward language.

He felt almost calm about it. The signal had a way of spreading calm along with the mathematics. This was how they did it, he realized. This was how Dylan had agreed to this. The signal itself was persuasive. It carried the logic of inevitability. It showed you the mathematics and the mathematics made sense and then you were making decisions based on logic that was no longer entirely your own.

But they had asked, somehow. They had sent a signal instead of just taking him. They had opened a channel and waited to see if he would listen.

His mother let go of his father's shoulders and walked to Cass. She put her arms around him, and he felt her shaking. Not shaking the way his father was shaking. Shaking with the effort of holding something together that wanted to fall apart.

"I'm sorry," she said into his hair. "I'm so sorry."

"I know," Cass said.

The needle behind his left eye moved again. One final pulse, like a countdown beginning. The signal was very clear now. Very large. It filled his skull with mathematics and inevitability and something that might have been compassion if compassion could exist in the language of pure geometry.

His mother held him tighter.

And in the basement of the house, surrounded by the bronze bells his father had spent his life learning to make, Cass felt himself beginning to change — not yet physically, but in the spaces between neurons, in the gaps between thought and word, in the part of himself that was already learning to hear the sound of something vast and alien thinking in the language of mathematics and light.

The needle pulsed.

The signal expanded.

And the boy his parents had raised began to become something they would never fully understand.
