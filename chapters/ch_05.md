# Chapter 5: The Frequency of Integration

The pod released him at 0400 hours.

Cass didn't know the time. Didn't know if it was morning or the middle of whatever passed for night on a ship that lived in vacuum. But his body knew. His bones knew. The frequency behind his left eye had been ticking in a steady rhythm for what felt like hours—three-four. Five. Three-four. Five—and then it stopped. And in the stopping, something in his chest released.

The membrane dissolved. Not like a door opening. Like water evaporating. One moment he was suspended in the medium, the next his skin was open to air—recycled air, cool, tasting of copper and salt. His lungs didn't remember how to process it.

He fell.

The floor of the tank was six feet below. His body hit hard enough to crack the breath out of him. Not enough to break anything. Exactly enough to make the impact felt.

Cass lay there. His fingers were webbed halfway now—thin membranes between knuckle and knuckle, iridescent when he held them up to the dim blue-green glow of the laboratory panels. His nails had gone dark, almost black. There was a taste in his mouth that wasn't copper anymore. Something older. Mineral. Stone.

He tried to stand.

His legs worked. His balance was wrong. The gravity on the ship was different from what his inner ear had been calibrated to in his first life—whatever that mattered now. He braced himself against the tank edge and pulled himself up, the webbing between his fingers stretching, not tearing. It was strong. It was part of him.

The laboratory was quiet except for the low hum of the suspension pods. Six of them in a row along the far wall. Cass could see through the translucent sides—shapes suspended in medium, barely moving. He didn't look long enough to identify them. Didn't want to know if they were drowning or if drowning was the wrong metaphor for whatever this was.

0.3̅ was at the viewing console, her massive form hunched over the interface. One of her six eyes was fixed on the tanks. The others moved independent of it, tracking something Cass couldn't see. When she noticed him, the eye swiveled.

The telepathic signal arrived fractionally after. Not words. Not exactly. But his mother's voice, rendered in mathematical precision, teal cascading through his visual field like northern lights: *Welcome back, little useful thing. Let's assess what you've become.*

Cass's new antennae—the chitinous nubs that had grown from his temples during the suspension—resonated with the signal. The frequency behind his left eye shifted. Changed. Synced.

It hurt.

Not the sharp shock of initial implantation. This was deeper. Like his skull was a bell and she was ringing it from the inside, and the sound was a color that didn't have a human name, and the color tasted like math, and the math was saying: *How does your nervous system respond to cascade-frequency modulation? How quickly can you shift baseline? What is your processing depth?*

"I—" Cass started to say. But his throat wasn't entirely human anymore. The sound came out fractured. Three frequencies at once. His mouth, and something underneath it. Something that wanted to speak in light.

She flashed colors at him. OrangeRed. Green. Something that looked like a question mark rendered in bioluminescence.

The translation arrived in fragments. Not through his ears. Through the space behind his eyes where the implant had woven itself into the architecture of his brain. *Acceptable baseline. Motor control adequate. Let's move to phase two.*

Cass's stomach tightened. He'd heard about phase two from the other humans during whatever window they'd had before the suspension. Xi Pei, whispering from his pod: *They start pulling you out more often. Showing you things. Testing response.*

"What's phase two?"

0.3̅'s entire body rippled with color. The other two cephalopods in the lab—Delta Del Squared and the smaller one whose name sounded like electrical discharge—shifted their positions. The smaller one's colors turned sharp. Predatory. Like she was hoping the answer to his question would be something that required teeth.

*You'll meet the collective.* His grandmother's voice, patient and warm. Warm the way a heat lamp is warm to something pinned beneath it. *The other aquatics aboard station are curious about the new processing capability we're developing. Thought-integration at this scale hasn't been attempted before. You should be proud.*

"Station?" Cass's legs weren't steady. He moved his weight carefully, finding the balance point. The webbing between his toes had spread too, he realized. He wasn't entirely standing on feet anymore. He was standing on something shaped like feet. *We're not on a ship?*

A tone of amusement rippled through the telepathic signal. Not kind. Not exactly cruel. The way you might find it funny that a plant was asking if it was soil.

*You're aboard Station Seven. The vessel brought you here for processing. Now begins the interesting part.* Her massive form shifted. One of her arms—a length of articulated muscle and suction cups the diameter of his head—extended toward him. Not threatening. Just reaching. *Come. We should begin integration. The collective's threshold for novelty is low. Best not to keep them waiting.*

Cass looked at her arm. Looked at the six tanks where the other humans were suspended in medium, in forced dreaming, in whatever the conversion process did to the mind while the body was being rebuilt.

"No," he said.

The laboratory went still. The word had come out clear. Had come out in something close to his own voice, though there was that harmonic underneath it now. That second voice that wasn't entirely his but was learning to be.

Delta Del Squared's colors flashed sharp and orange. There was something like excitement in it. Like he'd been hoping Cass would do exactly this.

0.3̅'s colors didn't change. That was worse. She was considering. Assessing.

*No?* Her voice came back gentler. Genuinely puzzled. *You've completed phase one successfully. The integration is already happening. The collective has been informed. The appointment is set. What is your objection?*

Cass's heart was hammering against ribs that didn't feel entirely normal anymore. He could feel something moving under his skin. Something that wasn't his heart. Something that had been growing in the suspension. He pushed the panic down. Pushed it into the same box where he'd pushed everything else—everything from his apartment, from the city, from the name his parents had given him.

"I want to know what the collective is," he said. "I want to know what happens in integration. I want—" The antennae behind his temples were vibrating. The frequency was accelerating. *Three-four. Five. Three-four. Five.* Faster now. Erratic. "I want to understand what's happening before I agree to it."

Delta Del Squared flashed orange again. The smaller one made a sound—not in the laboratory's air, but through the telepathic channel. A sound like something breaking.

0.3̅ was quiet for a long moment. One of her eyes turned away from Cass. Another rotated toward him. A third fixed on the suspension tanks.

*You're asking questions,* she said finally. *That's new. The others didn't ask. The first ones simply accepted. Some fought. One attempted suicide before we could revive her consciousness.* The colors that came with this were muted. Something like sorrow. Something like scientific disappointment. *But you're asking. That's interesting. That's exactly the kind of processing capability we hypothesized but didn't observe in earlier subjects.*

She moved to the viewing console. Her arms operated the interface with the fluid precision of someone using an extension of her own body—because it was. The biological screen responded to her touch like skin responding to thought. New images scrolled across the display: schematics. Neural maps. Something that looked like a mathematical equation rendered in three dimensions.

"The collective," 0.3̅ began, "is the telepathic network that binds all aquatic-caste cephalopods on this station. Approximately two hundred and thirty-seven individual minds, linked through biological resonance channels. When you integrate, you'll be able to perceive their thoughts. Not all of them. Your bandwidth is limited. But a significant frequency range will become accessible."

Cass's antennae were vibrating hard enough now that he could feel it in his teeth.

"And if I don't integrate?"

0.3̅'s colors cycled through something that might have been patience. Might have been the visual equivalent of a sigh.

*Then you remain isolated. Unable to communicate beyond this laboratory. Unable to participate in any meaningful way in the civilization we're building. You would be vermin that has learned speech but has nothing to say. Is that preferable?*

"I don't—" Cass stopped. Started again. "If I integrate, can I refuse other things? Can I refuse to—" He gestured at the suspension tanks. He couldn't make himself say what he was asking. *Can I refuse to participate in whatever you're doing to the others?*

Delta Del Squared flashed a color that was definitely laughter. The kind of laughter that involved teeth and hunger.

0.3̅'s colors went a shade of green he hadn't seen before. *That's a better question,* she said. *The answer is: probably. Collective membership comes with certain obligations, but individual aquatics retain autonomy in their research pursuits. If you develop the theoretical framework or practical capability to object meaningfully, that objection would carry weight.*

"You mean if I become smart enough, I can say no."

*I mean if you become something other than a novelty, your preferences might matter.* Her massive form turned, all her attention focusing on him now. Four eyes directly fixed. Two ranging the room. One watching the tanks. *That's what integration offers. Not escape. Not return to what you were. But the possibility of mattering. The possibility of having your preferences considered something other than the caprice of a tool.*

Cass stood in the blue-green glow of the laboratory. His webbed fingers were shaking. The frequency behind his left eye had stabilized at something faster than before. *Three-four. Five. Three-four. Five.* But now it was synced to something external. Now he could feel it pulsing from the collective itself, the way the entire station was breathing on that frequency, and beneath it, other frequencies, other thoughts, other minds—

He could almost hear them. Almost see them the way you see something in peripheral vision. Vast. Alien. Patient.

"When?" he asked.

*Three hours,* 0.3̅ said. *We'll need to prepare you. Get you into something more functional for the approach. And you should eat. Integration requires energy. Come.*

She extended that massive arm again. This time, it wasn't reaching. It was offering.

Cass took it.

---

The preparation chamber was smaller than the laboratory. Warmer too—designed for something that had been adapted to aquatic temperatures. The walls sweated condensation. The air was thick with salt and something organic he couldn't identify but his body recognized. His new body recognized it. His lungs opened wider to take it in.

0.3̅ had brought him here directly, her arm around him in something that might have been affection or might have been the way you'd carry a piece of equipment you didn't want to drop. The other two cephalopods had stayed in the laboratory, monitoring the suspension pods. Cass had caught Delta Del Squared's colors as they left—something orange and anticipatory. *Enjoy your elevation, little useful thing.*

Now 0.3̅ was moving him into a suit.

It wasn't like clothing. It was more like wearing a living thing. A second skin that wanted to integrate with his first skin. The jellyfish-like organisms that had accumulated on him during suspension began to pulse and migrate, responding to the presence of the suit. They flowed into it. They became part of it. And the suit—thin, iridescent, studded with small nodules that Cass realized were bioluminescent organs—began to bind to him at the cellular level.

The integration stung. Not pain exactly. Pressure. Like being slowly compressed and expanded simultaneously.

*This is necessary,* 0.3̅ said, her voice coming through the suit directly now, vibrating against his skin. *Aquatic cephalopods communicate primarily through bioluminescence and telepathy. Your vocal cords won't be sufficient in the presence. The suit will allow you to generate signals the collective will recognize. Primitive ones. But sufficient.*

"I can't speak to them," Cass managed. He was fighting to breathe while the suit settled.

*You can now.* Her colors rippled across the suit, teaching it. The suit rippled back, learning. *Not in your language. But you can express preference. Can signal integration status. Can ask questions. In a fashion.*

The suit was finished. Cass could feel it—the way he felt his own skin, the way he felt the vibrations in his bones. He looked down at himself. His hands were barely visible beneath the iridescence. When he moved them, the suit flickered with colors. Not random. Responsive. Like it was translating his intention into light.

*Move to the integration threshold,* 0.3̅ said.

The chamber had a door. Or what was being used as one. It was more membrane than barrier—a living thing that could be pushed through. On the other side, Cass could hear something. Not sound exactly. A vibration. A humming that felt pre-linguistic. The way the tuning fork behind his eye felt when he tried to think in the collective's frequency.

He pushed through the membrane.

The chamber beyond was vast. Cathedral-vast. Circular. The walls were living tissue, bioluminescent themselves, cycling slowly through colors that made Cass's eyes water to look at directly. And arranged in the chamber in what might have been concentric circles or might have been some three-dimensional pattern his human geometry couldn't parse—cephalopods. Dozens of them. Maybe more. Their colors were muted, waiting. Their eyes were fixed on the new arrival.

*The collective minds,* 0.3̅'s voice said, no longer coming from his companion but from everywhere and nowhere—from the walls themselves, perhaps, or from the frequency that was now thundering behind his left eye. *Let us begin.*

The colors hit him like a wave.

Not light. Language. Thought. Geometry rendered in mathematics rendered in light rendered in the electrical impulses that were still technically his nervous system, though the definition of "his" was becoming harder to hold. The colors came in layers—a base frequency that was the collective's baseline, the hum that kept them all synced and still, and then variations on top of it, individual minds within the whole, distinct enough to be separate but braided together in ways Cass didn't have vocabulary for.

*New processing capability. Assess.*

*Baseline primitive. Emotional integration incomplete.*

*Possible instability. Recommend observation protocol.*

*Interesting. The neural mapping suggests pre-existing adaptation. This one's mind was already fractured. The conversion may have created coherence rather than imposed structure.*

Cass was drowning. Or flying. The distinction seemed to matter less as the frequency grew stronger. His suit was flickering with colors—uncontrolled now, responding to the chaos in his nervous system. Panic? Fear? The collective's response was clinical. Observational. The way you might observe a chemical reaction, interested in the outcome but not invested in its comfort.

*Stabilize the subject,* a voice that might have been 0.3̅'s, or might have been the collective speaking with her mouth, said. *We need baseline cognitive function to proceed.*

Cass tried to speak. His mouth opened. Colors came out instead. Fractured light. His suit picked them up and amplified them—broadcasting his confusion across every frequency the collective was using.

And then—

Something shifted.

One of the larger cephalopods moved toward him. Not with aggression. With purpose. Its colors were different from the others. A deep indigo that seemed to absorb light rather than emit it. When it "spoke," the frequency behind Cass's left eye resonated with something like recognition.

*The young one is integrating faster than we modeled,* it said. *The fracture is not instability. It's adaptability. This one's consciousness was never meant to be whole in the first place.*

Another cephalopod responded—green and sharp: *Are you suggesting the primitive minds are actually optimized for transformation? That their fundamental fragmentation is an asset?*

*I'm suggesting,* the indigo one said, and its voice had a texture to it that the others didn't, something that tasted like age, like mathematics run through centuries, *that we've been asking the wrong question. We've been trying to elevate vermin to participation. We haven't considered that some vermin might already be evolved for exactly this purpose. That they might be the missing link in our own cognitive development.*

The collective's response was a wash of discordant colors. Argument. Consideration. Skepticism. All of it layered and precise, none of it in any language Cass could have spoken even with a human throat.

His suit flickered with a color that might have been acquiescence or might have been surrender. The frequency behind his left eye had stopped fluctuating. It had settled into something steady. *Three-four. Five. Three-four. Five.* Synced perfectly now with the collective's baseline. Not his rhythm anymore. Their rhythm. His heartbeat, their metronome.

*Remove him from the chamber,* 0.3̅ said. And then, softer, meant for him alone: *Rest now. The integration process requires integration. Your mind needs time to incorporate what it's experienced. Tomorrow we'll begin phase three.*

Cass didn't remember leaving the chamber. Didn't remember the walk back through the preparation room. Didn't remember much of anything except the frequency, the light, the sensation of his boundaries dissolving into something larger, and the smallest part of him—the part that had once been Dylan, that had once been an addict waking in strange rooms, that had once believed in the concept of being an individual person—that part whispered something that might have been relief.

He was useful now.

He was part of something.

He would never be alone again.

The pod they placed him in was different from before. Smaller. More like a cocoon than a tank. The medium was warmer. It tasted less like copper and more like something organic. Something alive. As his consciousness fractured and reformed along the lines the collective had drawn, Cass felt something growing inside his skull. Not the antennae. Something deeper. Something woven into the space between one thought and the next.

He let it happen.

Outside the pod, in the preparation chamber, 0.3̅ watched the readings on the console. The neural maps were changing in real-time. The baseline frequency was stabilizing across multiple quadrants. The integration was holding.

She pulsed colors that might have been satisfaction. And beneath that, colors that meant something else. Something complicated. The colors you'd flash if you were very good at your job and that job required you to be monstrous, and you'd made peace with the monstrosity so long ago that now it felt like mercy.

*He'll need to be watched,* Delta Del Squared said, manifesting at the chamber entrance. His colors were the orange of meat, of hunger barely restrained. *The indigo one took unusual interest. And if the young one truly is integrating at an accelerated rate—*

*Then he'll be invaluable,* 0.3̅ finished. *Or dangerous. Or both. Yes, I'll watch him. I'm already watching all of them.*

She turned her attention back to the suspension tanks in the main laboratory. The other humans were still dreaming their medicated dreams, still undergoing their slow conversion. Still becoming something other than what they had been. How many would survive? How many would integrate with Cass's speed? How many would have minds sharp enough to become interesting?

She had six to work with. Six chances. The odds were not favorable. But odds were only math, and she was very, very good at math.

The frequency pulsed through the station. Three-four. Five. Three-four. Five. The heartbeat of something vast and patient, learning to incorporate new rhythms, new textures, new possibilities from the vermin it had collected from that strange protected preserve where creatures built with dead materials and asked questions that had no place in a civilized mind.

Cass floated in his cocoon and dreamed in mathematics.
