# CHAPTER 4

The pod smelled like nothing—which was its own kind of smell. The absence of it. Like breathing in a void that had been sterilized of every human thing: sweat, skin oil, the salt residue of panic. Cass had lived in that nothing for an unknown amount of time, suspended in medium that felt like lukewarm milk, except his body wasn't touching anything. The sensation was worse for that. Like floating in a void that was somehow also crowding him.

Now he was awake.

The membrane above his face was transparent. Beyond it: the laboratory. The same laboratory he'd glimpsed before the pod sealed, but clearer now. His vision was different. Not better. Wider. The edges of his field felt expanded, as if his eyes had been recalibrated to take in more space at once. That wasn't right. That wasn't normal.

He tried to move his arm.

The signal left his brain. He felt it traveling down his spine, into his shoulder, coursing through the nerves of his arm like a current looking for a path. It reached the muscle and died there. His arm remained exactly where it had been.

The panic arrived late, which was worse than if it had arrived fast. There was a moment of not-understanding before the full shape of it assembled: he was paralyzed, he was sealed in a transparent pod, he was alive and conscious and utterly without recourse.

He did not scream. Some part of him wanted to. The signal tried to leave his spine multiple times—contract the diaphragm, force air through the vocal cords, make noise—and each time it dissolved somewhere between intention and execution.

Instead he watched.

The laboratory was vast. The ceiling curved away into shadow. Bioluminescent panels glowed softly along the walls—organic crescents that pulsed with a rhythm that matched nothing in nature. Banks of pods stretched in rows, each one translucent, each one containing something. Some contained what looked like flesh. Some contained shapes that didn't have names. One contained what might have been a partial skeleton, luminous and moving slightly, as if still breathing.

His pod was one of three that held human shapes.

In the first, a woman floated. Dark hair, pale skin. Her eyes were open. Staring at the ceiling. Staring through it. The implant nubs were visible at her temples—bronzed, chitinous, erupting from the skin like they were growing outward rather than being attached to it. They caught the bioluminescent light and held it.

In the third pod, a man. Asian. The nubs on his temples were more advanced. Longer. The bronze had begun to develop a sheen, a surface complexity that suggested crystalline growth. His eyes were closed.

Cass's own temples itched.

That was the moment he felt it—the sensation that had been subliminal until he focused on it. A pressure behind his eyes. Not pain yet. The promise of pain. The way a guitar string feels the moment before you turn the tuning key, tension building, not yet at the breaking point. The feeling behind his left eye was sharper. More insistent. It had a rhythm to it. A pulse. Three-four time, then five, then back to three. Musical in a way nothing in his body should be musical.

He tried to reach his temple with his hand.

The signal left his brain. This time it made it farther. His hand trembled. His shoulder blade contracted. His arm rose three inches and fell back.

The effort exhausted him. His breath came shallow and fast, which was strange because his lungs were moving, his diaphragm was firing, but the pod's medium was in his mouth and nose and there was no air. He was breathing liquid. It cycled through his lungs. It tasted like the inside of a metal cup. It tasted like the water that sits in a vase of flowers too long.

His brain understood that he should be drowning.

His lungs understood something else.

The laboratory door opened. The figure that entered was not human. The shock of it bypassed thought and went straight to autonomic response—his heart rate spiked, his pupils dilated, his skin flooded with sweat that mixed immediately with the pod medium. The signal for flight or fight left his brain and died in his muscles, unrequited.

The creature was enormous. Even bent to fit through the doorway, it had to be thirty feet tall, shoulders that eclipsed the frame. Tentacles. Seven of them visible. The color was a shifting spectrum—teal, blue-green, hints of purple at the edges. As the creature moved, the colors accelerated, rippling across the surface of its body in patterns that made Cass's eyes hurt to watch. They were too fast. Meaningless. Or not meaningless—the ripples had intention, they were clearly *communication*, but they were operating at a frame rate his nervous system couldn't process.

It had six eyes. All of them were open.

The creature moved to the computer terminal that controlled the pods. Its tentacles extended toward the interface—not just grasping, but *interfacing*, flowing over the surface like the machine was made of clay and the tentacle was reshaping it. The colors accelerated. Information exchanged. Cass couldn't read it. Nobody could read it. It was pure mathematics rendered in light, and his brain was not equipped to parse it.

The creature turned. All six eyes locked onto Cass's pod.

The signal was clear even without understanding the light patterns. Recognition. Assessment. The same expression Cass had seen on his father's face once when he'd opened a cardboard box that had been delivered to their house—not the kind of box you ordered, but the kind you find in a storage unit that belonged to a stranger. The expression of finding something you weren't sure how to categorize.

One of the eyes closed. Then another. Then another, until only one remained open, pointed directly at Cass.

The pain behind Cass's left eye intensified. Not a spike—a ramp. Steady increase. Like someone was turning a tuning key inside his skull, tightening the string that was supposedly a major nerve, tension building, building. He tried to move his hand to his temple again. This time his hand moved. The increment was small but real. His fingers traced the edge of his temple and found something they shouldn't find.

Something was growing there.

It was warm. Solid. Roughly chitinous to the touch, like a beetle's carapace, but it was *part of him*. Not grafted on. Not implanted. Growing from the inside out, pushing through the skin. His fingernail caught on a ridge where it erupted from his temple and the touch sent a cascade of white light through his visual field.

The pain spiked past ramp into something else. Something crystalline. Something beautiful and unbearable.

The creature made a sound—no, not a sound. Below sound. Something his inner ear felt rather than heard. A frequency that registered as color in his peripheral vision, as taste on his tongue. Copper. Ozone. Something metallic and alive. The creature was making *noise* and his body was receiving it as language, which meant the implants were working, which meant he was starting to interface with a species that was currently studying him the way a researcher studies an interesting bacterial strain.

The single eye watching him rippled with what might have been curiosity. What might have been hunger. Colors cascaded across the creature's skin again—the communication patterns accelerating, intensifying. Multiple tones arriving simultaneously. The pain behind Cass's left eye responded by reaching a frequency he'd never experienced before—sharp and crystalline and somehow musical, three-four time, five, back to three. Bronze. Incessant. The sound that would accompany the end of his life if the end of his life arrived in physical form.

The creature turned away.

The pod's medium began to drain. The sensation of it receding from his face was wrong in every way—gravity asserting itself, his body suddenly aware of weight it hadn't felt in however long, his lungs understanding that they were full of liquid and that the liquid was leaving and that air existed but wasn't arriving yet. The panic came then. Late but absolute. His body spasmed. Nothing responded. His lungs convulsed. The medium cycled back in, out. In, out. His throat muscles tried to gag, tried to reject, failed.

The medium cleared from his nose. From his mouth. Air rushed in. His lungs opened. The pain behind his left eye modulated into something almost tolerable—the tuning key stopping its turn, the tension holding steady rather than climbing.

He gasped. Actually gasped. The air was cold and tasted like metal and something else, something organic and faintly putrid. He gasped again. His lungs still functioned. His throat still worked. The signals were finally connecting, the wiring reestablishing itself from brain to body.

The creature spoke.

The voice was his grandmother's voice—not in actuality, but in the way his brain received it. Warm. Familiar. Asking him something in Mandarin, asking where he'd been, why he hadn't called, wasn't he hungry? His grandmother had been dead for twelve years. She had never left their village. She had never been on a space station or in a laboratory. She had never spoken to him across the void of what his implant was translating as language.

The creature—0.3̅, the implants were telling him now, that was the name, that was the mathematical frequency, the teal patterns meant *Repeating Remainder Three*—made another attempt at his grandmother's voice but the translation was breaking down. The Mandarin fragmented. The warmth stayed. The hunger behind it was becoming visible to him in ways it hadn't been before.

"You are awake," his grandmother said, except it wasn't his grandmother and it wasn't speaking in words anymore. It was speaking in *pressure*, in color, in the tuning fork sensation behind his left eye. "The medium will continue. Your integration will progress. You will find the process uncomfortable for some time. This is expected. This is acceptable."

Cass tried to respond. The signal left his brain. His mouth opened. No sound came out. Just medium, just fluid, just the sensation of drowning on dry land.

"Your neural substrate shows interesting properties," his grandmother continued. "More fragile than the previous specimens. More chaotic. The attunement markers suggest incomplete baseline cognitive organization. This is fascinating. This suggests you may have experienced significant neurological disruption in your natural state."

The pod began to refill.

Cass tried to move. His hand rose farther this time—all the way to his chest. His fingers flexed. The motion was jerky, wrong, like puppetry, like something else was operating the strings. But it was *motion*. It was the return of signals that had been severed.

The medium rose. His neck. His chin. The cold receding again, the sensation of drowning returning. His lungs cycled. The pain behind his left eye settled into something rhythmic, something he was beginning to understand as a signature, a frequency, a constant note that would define the rest of whatever came next.

"You will be useful," his grandmother said. The voice was fading. The pod was becoming opaque again, the medium thickening, the light outside dimming as if the laboratory itself was moving away. "Usefulness is the highest form of integration we can offer vermin. Accept this gift. Become what you are capable of becoming."

The pod sealed.

The silence was absolute.

Not the absence of sound—the laboratory had sound, he could feel it in his bones, the low-frequency hum of machinery, the thrum of the ship's systems below it, the constant drone of something vast and alive. This was the absence of the creature's voice. The absence of his grandmother. The absence of anything that acknowledged his existence.

Cass floated. The medium supported him. The tuning fork sensation behind his left eye continued its rhythm. Three-four. Five. Three-four. Five. A pulse. A pattern. A frequency that belonged to something other than himself, now being generated from inside his own skull.

He thought—not in words, but in the shape of thought—about his apartment in the city. The morning his landlord had finally evicted him. The cardboard box of belongings he'd carried to his sister's house three years ago and never picked up. The morning he'd woken to the sound of his sister crying on the phone. The moment before the light hit him from above, and the world became something else.

The memory didn't hurt anymore.

That was what made the pain behind his left eye almost bearable. The recognition that he could lose the memory and not miss it. That if he'd been attached to something real before, that attachment was already breaking. That the person he'd been was a draft, a preliminary sketch, a first attempt at a design that had never quite worked.

The creature—the scientist—had called him useful.

Cass had been called many things. Failure. Addict. Waste. Disappointment. Never useful. Never integral. Never essential in any way that mattered.

The frequency shifted. Three-four. Five. Three-four. Five. Three-four.

It was almost like music.

It was almost like coming home.
